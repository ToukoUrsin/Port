## 1. The Workflow: Event to Published Article

### 1.1 At the Event

**What a reporter actually does at an event:**

- **Phone as primary tool.** Smartphone serves triple duty: audio recorder, camera, and note-taking device. The built-in Voice Memo app (iOS) or equivalent is the most common recording tool. Many reporters also run a dedicated transcription app (Otter.ai, Good Tape) simultaneously.
- **Physical notebook still common.** Reporters take handwritten notes alongside recordings. The notebook captures observations the recorder cannot: who sat where, what the room felt like, body language, side conversations.
- **Photos on phone.** For local news, the reporter IS the photographer. iPhone/Android cameras have largely replaced DSLRs for daily news work. Dedicated photojournalists at larger outlets still use mirrorless cameras (Fujifilm X-T4, Canon EOS R3, Sony Alpha series), but the phone dominates local coverage.
- **Live social updates.** Many reporters post to X/Twitter or their publication's social accounts from the event itself — short updates, photos, key quotes.

**Recording methods in practice:**

- Phone's built-in voice recorder (free, always available)
- Otter.ai running on phone for real-time transcription
- TapeACall for phone interviews ($10/mo, records via three-way call merge)
- Dedicated digital recorders (Zoom H1n, Sony ICD series) used by investigative/audio journalists
- Video recording on phone for broadcast or social clips

### 1.2 Between Event and Writing

**The gap most people don't think about:**

- Travel time back to newsroom or home office (20-60 min typical for local reporters)
- Reviewing notes and recordings
- Making follow-up calls — getting the quote you missed, confirming a number, reaching the person who left early
- Transcribing key portions of recordings (AI has dramatically cut this from hours to minutes)
- Checking documents, public records, prior coverage for context

**This phase takes 30 minutes to several hours** depending on story complexity. For breaking news, it happens in the car or is skipped entirely — reporter files from the scene.

### 1.3 Writing the Article

**How journalists structure articles:**

The **inverted pyramid** remains the dominant structure for hard news:

1. **Lede** (lead paragraph): The most important fact in ~25 words. Answers as many of the 5 Ws (who, what, when, where, why) as possible in one sentence.
2. **Nut graf** (second/third paragraph): Why this matters. The context. Answers questions the lede raised.
3. **Supporting details** in descending order of importance. Quotes interspersed.
4. **Background/context** toward the bottom.
5. **Least essential details** at the end (can be cut by editors without losing the story).

This structure originated in the telegraph era — if the signal dropped, the essential information had already transmitted. It persists because editors can cut from the bottom to fit space.

**Key conventions:**
- Use "said" for attribution, almost always. Not "stated," "declared," "opined."
- Attribution goes mid-sentence or at end, not beginning. "The project will cost $2 million, Smith said" — not "Smith said the project will cost $2 million."
- Short sentences. Active voice. No adjectives that inject opinion.
- AP Stylebook is the US industry standard (numbers, dates, titles, abbreviations). Reuters has its own guide for international wire writing.
- Most hard news stories run 300-800 words. Reuters recommends keeping most stories in this range.

**Where they write:**

- **Google Docs** is the most common drafting tool across newsrooms of all sizes. Real-time collaboration, comment threads for editor feedback, version history.
- Stories are drafted in Docs, then copy-pasted or imported into the CMS for formatting and publication.
- Some reporters write directly in the CMS (WordPress, Arc XP, Newspack), but Docs remains dominant for the drafting phase.
- For breaking news, reporters may file directly into the CMS or even dictate to an editor by phone.

**How long does writing take?**

| Story type | Writing time | Total time (reporting + writing) |
|---|---|---|
| Breaking news brief (200-400 words) | 15-30 min | 30 min - 2 hours |
| Standard daily news (500-800 words) | 30-60 min | 2-6 hours |
| Feature/enterprise (1,500-3,000 words) | 2-8 hours | Days to weeks |
| Investigation | Days of writing | Weeks to months |

A professional journalist familiar with the topic can produce a clean 1,000-word first draft in 30-60 minutes. The research and reporting beforehand is what takes time.

**Deadlines:**
- Daily newspapers: stories due by 3-4 PM for next-day print. Online stories filed throughout the day.
- Weekly papers: if Friday publication, deadline is Tuesday or Wednesday.
- Breaking news: measured in minutes. AP reporters file a "first-lede writethru" within minutes, then update throughout the day.
- TV news: story must be recorded, edited, and delivered before the newscast starts.

### 1.4 The Edit Process

**At a well-staffed newsroom (increasingly rare):**

1. **Reporter submits draft** (via Google Docs or CMS)
2. **Assignment editor / section editor** reads for:
   - Is the lede right? (Most common feedback: "you buried the lede")
   - Are there enough sources? ("Need another source on this")
   - Is it fair and balanced?
   - Does the structure work?
   - Are claims supported?
3. **Reporter revises** based on editor feedback (may involve more reporting)
4. **Copy editor** reviews for:
   - Grammar, spelling, AP Style compliance
   - Fact-checking basics (names, titles, dates, geographic locations)
   - Headline writing
   - Photo captions
5. **Page designer / digital producer** formats for print or web

**How many rounds of editing?**

- Routine daily story: 1-2 rounds (editor review + copy edit)
- Feature/enterprise: 2-4 rounds (substantive edits, then line edits, then copy edit)
- Investigation: Many rounds over weeks

**What happens at small papers (the norm in 2026):**

- Reporter writes, self-edits, publishes. No separate editor review.
- Or: reporter writes, one editor gives a quick read (5-10 minutes), publishes.
- No copy editor. No fact-checker. The reporter is responsible for all of it.
- Result: more errors in published articles. This has been extensively documented.

### 1.5 Fact-Checking: Three Models

1. **Magazine model:** Dedicated fact-checker independently verifies every claim, quote, and number before publication. Used by The New Yorker, major magazines. Very expensive.
2. **Newspaper model:** Journalist is responsible for their own facts. Editors push back on claims that "don't sound right." Copy editors check basic facts (spelling of names, titles, locations). This is the dominant model.
3. **Hybrid model:** Long/complex pieces get magazine-style checking; daily news gets newspaper model. Used by some larger outlets.

At small/local papers in 2026, there is effectively **no fact-checking layer at all** beyond the reporter's own diligence.

## 2. Tools Journalists Use in 2026

### 2.1 Recording and Transcription

| Tool | Cost | Accuracy | Best for | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Otter.ai** | Free tier / $16.99/mo Pro | ~90% (real-time) | Live meetings, interviews | Industry standard. Real-time transcription. English-focused. |
| **Good Tape** | Free (3x30min/mo) / Pro paid | 90-95% | Interview transcription | Built by Danish journalists. 100+ languages. EU-hosted, privacy-first. No AI training on files. 2.5M users, 10M+ files transcribed. |
| **Trint** | $52-60/user/mo | 90-95% | Newsroom teams | Built for newsrooms. 40+ language transcription, 50+ translation. Collaborative quote verification. Expensive — viable mainly for large outlets. |
| **Rev.com** | $0.25/min AI, $1.99/min human | 90% AI / 99% human | High-accuracy needs | Human transcription option is the key differentiator. |
| **Descript** | $15-30/mo | ~91% | Podcast/video journalists | Text-based audio/video editing — edit the transcript and it edits the media. |
| **TapeACall** | ~$10/mo | N/A (recording only) | Phone call recording | Records phone calls via three-way merge. No transcription built in. |
| **Phone Voice Memos** | Free | N/A | Quick recordings | Always available. No transcription. |
| **Google Pinpoint** | Free (for journalists) | Varies | Document analysis | Part of Google's Journalist Studio. Searches across documents, images, audio. |

**The big shift (2024-2026):** Transcription used to be the most hated journalism task — manually transcribing a 1-hour interview took 4-6 hours. AI transcription tools have collapsed this to minutes. This is the single most impactful AI adoption in journalism so far.

### 2.2 AI Tools Already in Newsroom Use

**What's actually being used (not hype):**

- **Transcription** — The #1 AI use case. Nearly universal adoption. Otter.ai, Good Tape, Trint, built-in Zoom transcription.
- **Public meeting monitoring** — AI tools transcribe and summarize city council, school board, and zoning meetings that reporters cannot physically attend.
  - **Assembly (Hearst):** Transcribed 13,119 hours (547 days) of government meetings between May 2024 and April 2025. Reporters can "chat" with meeting transcripts.
  - **LocalLens:** Searchable database of public meetings with keyword alerts and annotated transcripts.
  - **SeeGov, Civic Sunlight:** Additional municipal meeting transcription tools.
- **Headline optimization** — Hearst's **Producer-P** (Slack-based, GPT-4 powered) suggests SEO titles, headlines, URLs, and notification summaries.
- **Chartbeat** — Analytics tool now includes AI-driven headline suggestions.
- **Story rewriting for syndication** — Reach (UK's largest regional publisher) uses **Gutenbot** to rewrite stories for different websites in its network.
- **Automated earnings/sports reports** — AP uses **Wordsmith** (Automated Insights) to generate routine earnings reports and sports summaries from structured data. Running for years.
- **Express.de's "Klara Indernach"** — AI bot that authored 1,500+ stories, accounting for 10% of stories read on the German tabloid.
- **NYT custom AI** — Nearly 40 reporters use an in-house AI transcription and summarization tool. Also built a custom tool to track the "manosphere."
- **Google NotebookLM** — Used by journalists for summarizing transcripts, documents, and investigation data.

**What's NOT being used (despite hype):**

- AI does NOT write publishable news stories at quality outlets. It generates drafts of routine/formulaic content (earnings, sports scores, weather) but NOT reported news.
- AI-generated transcripts still require human review — they are not publishable as-is.
- Reporters are skeptical. Audiences are skeptical. 87% of newsroom leaders say they are "fully or somewhat transformed" by AI, but the actual transformation is mostly in backend/workflow, not in public-facing content.

**2026 trajectory:** Agentic AI for end-to-end workflow automation (not content generation) is the next phase. Well-resourced newsrooms are building custom tools, not adopting off-the-shelf AI writing assistants.

### 2.3 CMS Platforms

| CMS | Target | Cost | Key features | Used by |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **WordPress** (self-hosted) | Everyone | Free (hosting costs) | Ubiquitous, huge plugin ecosystem | Majority of news sites worldwide |
| **Newspack** | Local/nonprofit news | $750+/mo (revenue-based) | WordPress-based, built-in membership/newsletters/ads, SEO, analytics. Shared tech team. No setup fees. | 300+ news sites. Backed by Google News Initiative, Knight Foundation, Lenfest. |
| **Ghost** | Small publishers, indie journalists | $0 self-hosted / $15-199/mo hosted | Native newsletters, membership/subscriptions (0% revenue cut), Stripe integration, open source | Stanford Review, Madison Minutes, indie journalists |
| **Arc XP** | Large publishers | $50K-3M+/year | Enterprise suite: Composer (authoring), WebSked (planning), PageBuilder (layout), subscriber management | Washington Post, Boston Globe, Le Parisien, Infobae, Irish Times |
| **WordPress VIP** | Enterprise | Premium pricing | Managed WordPress for high-traffic publishers | Major media companies |

**The practical reality:** Most journalists never think about their CMS. They write in Google Docs, an editor reviews it there, and then someone (often the reporter themselves at small outlets) copy-pastes it into WordPress and adds a photo. The CMS is for publishing, not writing.

### 2.4 Collaboration and Communication

- **Slack** — Dominant internal communication tool in newsrooms of all sizes. Channels for #breaking-news, #story-pitches, individual beats.
- **Email** — Still used for source communication, PR pitches, external contacts.
- **Phone** — Still the primary tool for source interviews. Not going away.
- **Zoom / Google Meet** — For remote interviews and editorial meetings.
- **WhatsApp / Signal** — For sensitive source communication (especially investigative reporters).
- **Google Docs comments** — The de facto editing feedback mechanism. Editor leaves comments on the draft; reporter resolves them.
- **In-person** — At outlets with physical newsrooms, the most important conversations still happen face-to-face.

### 2.5 Photo and Media

**At local papers (one-person operation):**
- iPhone/Android camera. Period.
- Photos transferred directly from phone to CMS upload.
- No photo editing beyond basic cropping.
- Reporter takes 5-20 photos at an event, picks 1-3 for the story.

**At larger outlets:**
- Dedicated photojournalists with mirrorless cameras (Fujifilm, Canon, Sony).
- Photos filed via camera-to-cloud technology.
- Photo desks manage selection, cropping, captioning.
- Wire services (AP, Reuters, AFP) provide stock news photos.

## 3. Small/Local Newsroom Reality

### 3.1 The Numbers (2025 Medill State of Local News Report)

- **213 news desert counties** in the US (up from 206 in 2024, ~150 twenty years ago)
- **1,524 counties** with only ONE remaining news source
- **~50 million Americans** have limited to no access to local news
- **136 newspapers closed** in 2025 alone (>2 per week)
- **~3,500 newspapers** closed in the past 20 years
- **270,000+ newspaper jobs** lost since 2005 (75%+ decline)
- **Fewer than 1,000 daily newspapers** remain in the US (first time ever in 2025)
- **Web traffic to top 100 newspapers** down 45%+ in past 4 years
- **78,800 total newspaper jobs** remaining as of December 2025 (down 82% from 450,000 in 1990)

### 3.2 Typical Local Newsroom Staffing

**Small daily newspaper (2026):** 5-15 editorial staff total. This includes reporters, editors, photographers, and digital producers. Many positions have been combined.

**Small weekly newspaper:** 1-5 people. Often 1-2 doing everything.

**Hyperlocal digital-only:** Frequently **one person** — the founder/editor/reporter/photographer/social media manager/ad sales person.

**What "one person does everything" actually means:**

A typical day for a solo hyperlocal publisher:
1. Morning: Check email, social media, police blotter, court calendar for news tips
2. Attend a city council meeting or school board hearing (2-3 hours)
3. Take photos at the meeting with phone
4. Drive back, transcribe key quotes using Otter.ai or Good Tape
5. Write the story (30-60 min)
6. Self-edit (no one else to check it)
7. Upload to WordPress, add photos, write headline, set SEO tags
8. Post to Facebook, X/Twitter, maybe Instagram
9. Respond to reader comments and emails
10. Work on tomorrow's story
11. Maybe sell an ad or manage a subscription campaign

**This person produces 1-3 stories per day**, attends public meetings that no one else covers, and does it for significantly less money than their predecessors at legacy papers.

### 3.3 The Loss of Editors

**The concrete impact:**

- Copy editors have been cut more than any other newsroom category. Nearly a third of copy editors working at US dailies in 2007 are gone.
- Fewer copy editors + more content to produce = documented spike in published errors.
- No copy editor means no second pair of eyes on headlines — resulting in more embarrassing/misleading headlines.
- No assignment editor means reporters choose their own stories with less strategic direction.
- No fact-checking layer means errors that would have been caught go to publication.

**The quality equation:** Papers are producing MORE content (web, social, newsletters) with FEWER people, at the SAME or LOWER quality standard.

### 3.4 Digital Startups Filling the Gap

- **300+ local news startups** launched in the past 5 years across virtually every US state
- **80% are digital-only**
- **853 local news sites** across 52 networks now provide original reporting (Medill 2025)
- Most are funded by a mix of: advertising, memberships/subscriptions, philanthropic grants (Knight Foundation, Google News Initiative, American Journalism Project), and founders' personal savings

## 4. The Reporter-Editor Relationship

### 4.1 What Good Editorial Feedback Looks Like

**Common editor feedback on drafts (in order of frequency):**

1. **"You buried the lede."** — The most important fact is in paragraph 3 or 4 instead of paragraph 1. The single most common piece of editorial feedback.
2. **"Need another source."** — Story relies on one perspective. Editor wants a counterpoint, an expert, or an affected person.
3. **"What's the nut graf?"** — The story doesn't explain why the reader should care. Missing the "so what."
4. **"This needs more context."** — What happened before? What does this number mean in context?
5. **"Tighten this up."** — Too many words. Cut 30%.
6. **"The quote doesn't say anything."** — Reporter included a quote that's generic/meaningless. Find a better one.
7. **"Attribute this."** — A claim is stated as fact without a source.
8. **"I don't understand this paragraph."** — If the editor doesn't understand it, the reader won't.
9. **"Find the human."** — Story is all policy/numbers, no real person affected.
10. **"Start over."** — Nuclear option. Story's framing is fundamentally wrong.

**How feedback is delivered:**
- Google Docs inline comments (most common in 2026)
- Slack messages
- In-person conversation at the editor's desk (traditional)
- Track changes in Word (legacy outlets)

### 4.2 Edit Cycle Timing

| Story type | Editor turnaround | Rounds | Total edit time |
|---|---|---|---|
| Breaking news | Minutes | 0-1 | Under 30 min |
| Daily news | 30 min - 2 hours | 1-2 | 1-4 hours |
| Feature | 1-3 days | 2-4 | Days to a week |
| Investigation | Ongoing | Many | Weeks |

**At small papers:** Edit cycle is often just the reporter re-reading their own story once before hitting publish. Or the editor gives a 5-minute skim and says "looks fine."

### 4.3 How Layoffs Changed the Dynamic

- Many reporters now have no regular editor at all. They file stories that go directly to publication.
- Where editors remain, they are overloaded — managing 8-12 reporters instead of 3-4, reviewing 15-20 stories per day instead of 5-8.
- The "teaching" function of editing (mentoring young reporters, coaching story structure) has largely disappeared at understaffed outlets.

## 5. Citizen Journalism and Community Reporting

### 5.1 Platforms for Citizen/Community Journalism

| Platform | Model | Scale | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Patch** | AI-aggregated hyperlocal newsletters + 85 human journalists | 30,000 US communities, 3M+ subscribers | LLMs scrape local sites, social media, official sources. Twice-weekly or daily newsletters by zip code. |
| **TAPinto** | Franchise model | 90+ franchisees, 17+ years | Each franchisee publishes 1+ original local story/day. $1,000 startup + $750/mo licensing fee. SPJ ethics code required. |
| **Nextdoor** | Social platform with news feed | Nationwide | Not journalism, but where many people get local information. |
| **Facebook local groups** | Community-run | Ubiquitous | Unstructured, unverified, but often the only "news" source in news deserts. |
| **Substack** | Individual newsletters | Growing | Some local journalists have left papers to run paid Substack newsletters. |

### 5.2 How Community Reporters Differ from Professionals

| Dimension | Professional journalist | Community reporter |
|---|---|---|
| Training | J-school or newsroom apprenticeship | Short course (5-14 weeks) or self-taught |
| Ethics framework | Internalized AP/SPJ standards | Taught basics, less ingrained |
| Source network | Years of beat relationships | Local knowledge, personal connections |
| Verification | Systematic fact-checking habits | Less rigorous, learning |
| Legal knowledge | Understands libel, FOIA, shield laws | Often limited |
| Coverage scope | Assigned beats, breaking news | What they see in their neighborhood |
| Compensation | Salary or freelance rates | Stipends, volunteer, or small fees |

### 5.3 Training Programs for Citizen Journalists

- **Point Park University Citizen Reporting Academy** — Fully online certificate program. Graduates fill gaps in news deserts.
- **Minnesota Citizen Journalism University** — 5-week intensive online program. 45+ graduates, many now working as staff or stringers.
- **The Land (Cleveland)** — Graduated 100+ residents. Trains them to report from their neighborhoods, pairs them with professional journalists. Pays participants.
- **CUNY AI Community Engagement Lab** — 6-month program (2025). Focuses on AI tools for community media.
- **City Bureau (Chicago)** — Trains community members as "documenters" who attend and report on public meetings.

## 6. Finnish Journalism Specifically

### 6.1 The Finnish Media Landscape

**Structure:**
- Strong public broadcaster (**Yle**) — ~2,984 permanent employees in 31 locations. Funded by Yle tax (max 160 EUR/year per taxpayer). Budget ~500M EUR.
- One dominant national daily (**Helsingin Sanomat**, Sanoma Media)
- Two popular evening tabloids (Ilta-Sanomat, Iltalehti) — each reaching over half of adult population
- Strong regional press (**maakunta- ja paikallislehdet**) — 180+ local papers appearing 1-4 times/week
- 94% of Finnish municipalities covered by a local or city newspaper

**Key difference from US:** Finland's media ecosystem has NOT collapsed the way America's has. Regional papers remain viable (though under pressure). Public broadcasting (Yle) provides a baseline of local coverage that the US lacks entirely.

### 6.2 Yle's Local News Operation

- **31 regional editorial offices** across Finland
- Digital-first workflow: content published online throughout the day, then adapted for TV/radio
- **January 2025 cuts:** Yle announced closure of 309 posts (10% of workforce, 27M EUR savings). This affected regional operations.
- Yle remains the most trusted news source in Finland

### 6.3 STT (Finnish News Agency)

- Founded 1887 — one of the oldest news agencies in the world
- Only commercial news agency in Finland producing comprehensive real-time news
- Offers news in Finnish, Swedish, and English
- STT style specifics are internal editorial standards (not publicly documented like AP Stylebook)

### 6.4 Finnish Journalism Culture vs. US/UK

| Dimension | Finland | US | UK |
|---|---|---|---|
| Press freedom | #1 or top 3 globally (RSF) | ~40s-50s | ~20s-30s |
| Trust in news | Highest in Europe, ~70%+ | ~32% (Pew) | ~30-40% |
| Self-regulation | Council for Mass Media (JSN), est. 1968. Handles complaints free of charge. | No equivalent | IPSO (weaker) |
| Public broadcasting | Yle — strong, well-funded, trusted | NPR/PBS — marginal | BBC — strong but under pressure |
| Media literacy | World-leading. Taught in schools. | Minimal | Moderate |
| Transparency | Official documents public by default | FOIA (must request) | FOI Act (must request) |
| Nordic model | Part of "media welfare state" — state supports universally accessible communication | Free market, minimal state support | Mixed |

**Key Finnish characteristics:**
- **Institutionalized editorial freedom** — media companies explicitly guarantee editorial independence from owners
- **Self-regulation works** — JSN/CMM is respected. A JSN ruling that you breached ethics is taken seriously. The outlet MUST publish the ruling.
- **High media literacy** — Finnish schools teach critical media consumption. This produces an audience that demands quality.
- **~1,000 journalism jobs lost** between 2010-2020 as digital advertising revenues dropped ~35%

### 6.5 Finnish News Consumption (Reuters DNR 2025)

- Online news use: ~90% weekly reach (unchanged)
- Television news and printed press: slow decline continuing
- Finland remains one of the highest news-consuming countries in Europe
- High willingness to pay for news (relative to other countries)
