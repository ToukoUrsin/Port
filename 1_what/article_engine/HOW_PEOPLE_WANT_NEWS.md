# How People Communicate and Consume Local News (2026)

Deep research synthesis. Every claim below is sourced from named studies with real data.

---

## 1. HOW PEOPLE CURRENTLY SHARE AND FIND LOCAL NEWS

### 1.1 The Dominant Pattern: Word of Mouth Still Wins

The single most common way Americans get local news is from **friends, family, and neighbors**: 73% say they often or sometimes get local news this way (Pew Research, Jan 2024, n=5,146). This is higher than any media source. For local political news specifically, 70% get it from friends/family and 66% from local news outlets.

This is not new. What is new is that "word of mouth" now happens on digital platforms.

### 1.2 Facebook Local Groups

**Scale:** Over 70 million active Facebook Groups globally. 74% of Facebook users join groups to connect with others who share interests. Average user belongs to 8.5 groups (Facebook/Meta data, 2025).

**Engagement:** Community groups (hobbies, parenting, local interests) drive **50% more engagement** than branded Pages. Groups drive **15x more engagement** than Pages overall (Hootsuite/Buffer 2025 analysis).

**For news specifically:** 30% of U.S. adults regularly get news on Facebook -- more than any other social platform (Pew Research, June 2024). The share getting news from **online forums like Facebook groups increased from 38% in 2018 to 52% in 2024** -- a 14 percentage point jump in six years (Pew-Knight Initiative, 2024).

**What gets posted in local Facebook groups:** Service requests, event announcements, lost pets, recommendations, complaints about local issues, photos of local happenings, links to local news articles. The most engaged posts tend to be about immediate community concerns (safety, road closures, events).

**Demographic note:** Facebook's audience skews older. Millennials and young adults engage more through Marketplace, Groups, and Reels rather than traditional status updates or link posts (Buffer 2025).

### 1.3 Nextdoor

**Research base:** NYU Center for Social Media and Politics + University of Michigan, 2024. Sampled 30 Nextdoor neighborhoods, collected ~116,000 posts and ~164,000 comments.

**Most common post types (by volume):**
1. Posts seeking or offering services (most frequent)
2. Neighborhood logistics -- lost pets, transportation issues, events
3. Pleasantries -- holiday wishes, celebrating local weather
4. Sharing local news reports (identified as one of the "informative post types")

**Highest engagement:** Posts about perceived suspicious people or activities received the most comments per post, despite being only ~6% of total posts. Only 10.6% of posts overall express positive sentiment.

**Demographics:** Nextdoor neighborhoods are more likely to be in communities that are less dense, whiter, wealthier, older, and more educated (NYU/UMich 2024).

**News growth:** People who regularly get news on Nextdoor increased 5 percentage points in the past year, while news consumption on other platforms declined (Nextdoor 2025 data).

**Trust hierarchy on Nextdoor:** Neighbors and local community members were the most trusted source (36%), followed by friends and family (30%), online reviews (12%), social media (11%), and local news outlets (10%) (Nextdoor survey 2025).

### 1.4 WhatsApp Groups

**Global scale:** Over 800 million active WhatsApp groups globally. Average user is part of 18 groups. 41-57.5% of total message volume comes from group chats. Average group: 27 members (WhatsApp data via multiple sources, 2025).

**European penetration:** 82% of European mobile phone users use WhatsApp. Penetration by country: Spain 91%, Germany 81%, Italy/UK 40-50M users each.

**Finland:** 89% of Finns use WhatsApp (2022 survey). Over 1.4 million users across age groups.

**Voice notes:** 7 billion voice messages sent daily on WhatsApp globally. ~5% of all messages are voice notes. Usage varies sharply by age: 12% of 18-24 year olds send voice notes daily, while 63% of all smartphone users have never sent one (YouGov UK data).

**For community news:** WhatsApp groups function as informal news networks, especially in European communities. Parents groups, neighborhood groups, hobby/sports club groups, and school groups all serve as de facto local news channels. The platform's group structure makes it natural for sharing local events, warnings (weather, road closures), and community announcements.

### 1.5 Instagram and TikTok

**News consumption rates (Pew, June 2024):**
- Instagram: 16% of U.S. adults regularly get news there
- TikTok: 14% of U.S. adults regularly get news there

**Engagement:** TikTok average engagement rate 2.50% (highest of any platform in 2024). Instagram: 0.50%. Facebook: 0.15%.

**Key difference:** Instagram's network-driven approach amplifies collaborative content, with legacy media dominating the information ecosystem. TikTok's algorithm-driven feed prioritizes individual creators and novel content, democratizing content discovery and favoring independent influencers over legacy media.

**Local news on visual platforms:** Teens gravitate toward video-heavy platforms (YouTube, TikTok, Instagram) while shifting away from Facebook. For local news specifically, these platforms are more about ambient awareness than deliberate news consumption. Local events, restaurant openings, community happenings get shared as Reels/TikToks, but systematic local news coverage is minimal on these platforms.

**Among 18-24 year olds:** 44% say social media is their main news source (Reuters Institute 2025).

### 1.6 Twitter/X

**For local news, X is weak.** Pew Research found that local news data on Twitter was "so sparse that it was not as useful as hoped." Even what local news was shared on Twitter was often different from what local news outlets were actually covering.

14% of the general U.S. population uses X for news (though among X users, 69% say they primarily use it for news). X functions better for national/breaking news and for local organizations broadcasting information than for community-level local news sharing.

### 1.7 Reddit Local Subreddits

Reddit's user base skews young (18-29), male, and liberal, with deep interest in news. Local subreddits (e.g., r/LosAngeles with 282K+ members) function as community discussion forums where residents talk about local issues.

Local subreddits are best for: story ideas, community sentiment, discussion of local controversies, recommendations. They are weakest for: systematic news coverage, breaking news timeliness, demographic representativeness.

Key insight: "The more well-defined or smaller a subreddit community is, the more likely it is to be useful" (Cronkite News Lab).

### 1.8 Telegram in Nordic Countries

Finland has 1,851 active Telegram communities with a total audience of over 14.9M subscribers. Content skews toward crypto, jobs, study groups, and general discussion rather than local news. Telegram is not a primary local news channel in the Nordics -- WhatsApp and Facebook dominate that function.

## 2. WHAT PEOPLE ACTUALLY WANT FROM LOCAL NEWS

### 2.1 Topics: The Definitive Ranking (Pew Research, Jan 2024, n=5,146)

Percentage of U.S. adults who "often or sometimes" get news about each topic:

| Topic | Often or Sometimes | Often Only |
|-------|-------------------|------------|
| Weather | 90% | 68% |
| Crime | 77% | ~34% |
| Government & politics | 68% | -- |
| Traffic & transportation | 68% | -- |
| Arts & culture | 59% | -- |
| Economy | 59% | -- |
| Schools | 56% | -- |
| Sports | 54% | -- |

**Weather is king.** 68% "often" get weather news -- double the share for crime, the next most common topic.

**Crime interest is broad:** ~75%+ of adults are at least somewhat interested in details of specific crimes, safety tips, AND broader crime patterns.

**Schools are parent-driven:** Parents of children under 18 are about twice as likely as non-parents to often get news about local schools.

### 2.2 What People Miss When Local Newspapers Disappear

Research across multiple studies (Northwestern Medill, American Journalism Project, Notre Dame 2018) identifies what communities lose:

**Community connection:** Local papers told readers who got married, who passed away, who made the honor roll, who got arrested. They connected people through births, deaths, celebrations, and community events that nurture shared culture.

**Accountability:** When newspapers close, government inefficiency and borrowing costs increase (University of Notre Dame, 2018). No one attends school board meetings. No one digs into town budgets. No one asks hard questions of local officials.

**Small-problem coverage:** With fewer journalists, surviving papers focus only on big stories. Missing: the local stories that once inspired people to engage with others and solve small problems before they became big ones.

**Shared identity:** Strong community journalism engenders identity and engagement with others who live in the same vicinity.

### 2.3 Reuters Institute: Where Publishers Still Win (2025)

The Reuters Institute's 2025 Digital News Report (nearly 100,000 respondents, 48 countries) found:

- **84% of respondents** are at least somewhat interested in current information about their area
- **50% consumed local news within a week** (German data)
- **Around 90%** of people in Sweden and Finland accessed some type of local information in the last week

**Where news media still wins vs platforms:**
- Local politics and breaking news: People still prefer traditional local media
- Original reporting and investigative journalism

**Where platforms now win:**
- Local buying/selling: platforms beat news media by **23 percentage points**
- Community activities and events
- Transport information
- Services and recommendations

**Country matters enormously:** In Finland, where platformization is low and people go directly to publisher websites, people say news media is the best local information source. In Thailand, where platformization is high, people say platforms are best.

### 2.4 Trust and Willingness to Pay

**Trust:** 74% of Americans have "a lot" or "some" trust in their local news organizations. 85% believe local news outlets are at least somewhat important to their community (Pew 2024).

**Global trust:** 40% average trust globally -- flat for three years (Reuters Institute 2025). Finland: 67% (among highest globally). Hungary/Greece: 22% (lowest).

**Willingness to pay:** Despite high trust, few pay. Only 16% of Americans currently pay for a news subscription. Only 23% of those who pay for online news pay for a local/regional paper. When asked what should be free: weather (15%), safety (16%), and local news (16%) top the list.

**The trust-payment gap is the central paradox:** People value local news, trust it, believe it matters for their community -- and will not pay for it.

## 3. THE PSYCHOLOGY OF SHARING NEWS

### 3.1 Why People Share (Berger's STEPPS Framework + Academic Research)

Jonah Berger's research (Wharton, analysis of thousands of NYT articles) identified six drivers of sharing, known as STEPPS:

1. **Social Currency** -- sharing makes the sharer look good, informed, connected
2. **Triggers** -- environmental cues that keep the topic top-of-mind
3. **Emotion** -- high-arousal emotions (awe, anger, anxiety) drive sharing; low-arousal (sadness) suppresses it
4. **Public** -- visible behavior breeds imitation
5. **Practical Value** -- "news you can use" gets shared to help others
6. **Stories** -- narrative vessels carry information further than facts alone

### 3.2 Specific Data on News Sharing Behavior

- **73%** share online to meet others with shared interests (NYT Customer Insight Group study)
- **78%** share to communicate with people they otherwise wouldn't stay in touch with
- **68%** share to communicate who they are and what they care about
- **Negative news articles are 1.91x more likely to be shared** on social media than positive ones (Nature Scientific Reports, 2024, data from four US/UK news sites)
- But within shared content, **positive content with high arousal (awe)** is more viral than negative low-arousal content
- **People share news without reading it:** social media users frequently share based on headlines alone (Penn State University research)

### 3.3 Motivations Specific to News Sharing (Frontiers in Psychology, 2025)

A PLS-SEM study of Gen Z news sharing found significant drivers:
- Information seeking
- Socializing
- Status seeking
- Entertainment
- Fear of Missing Out (FoMO)
- Social significance of the content
- Audience relevance

"Pass-time" was the only tested motivation that did NOT significantly influence sharing intention.

### 3.4 The "I Saw Something" to "I Should Tell People" Gap

The friction between witnessing something newsworthy and actually sharing it involves:

**Competence barrier:** "I don't know how to write about this properly." People who are not writers self-censor because they fear their account won't be good enough.

**Legitimacy barrier:** "Who am I to report this?" Without a title (journalist, official), people doubt their authority to share news.

**Effort barrier:** Writing a coherent account takes time and cognitive effort. Speaking is 3-5x faster than typing for most people.

**Judgment fear:** "People will think I'm being dramatic / attention-seeking / wrong."

**Platform friction:** "Where would I even post this? My personal feed? A group? Who would see it?"

### 3.5 Voice Notes vs. Text: The Demographic Split

- **7 billion WhatsApp voice messages sent daily** globally
- **12% of 18-24 year olds** send voice notes daily
- **63% of all smartphone users** have never sent a voice note
- Younger users are more receptive to receiving voice notes
- Voice notes are popular for hands-free communication (driving, cooking, walking)
- In-app voice notes used by 62% of daily WhatsApp users

The voice-text split is generational but also situational. People use voice when typing is inconvenient, when emotion matters (tone carries meaning), or when the message is too long to type but too casual to call about.

## 4. COMMUNITY REPORTING MOTIVATIONS

### 4.1 What Motivates Community Contributors

**Intrinsic motivations (from within):**
- Sense of efficacy -- feeling they had an effect on their community
- Enjoyment of the activity itself
- Community pride and identity
- Desire to help neighbors be informed
- Self-expression

**Extrinsic motivations (external rewards):**
- Recognition and prestige in the community
- Social interaction and reciprocity
- Payment (where available)
- Building a reputation or portfolio

**Critical finding (motivation crowding):** Extrinsic motivators (e.g., payment) can undermine intrinsic motivation -- OR strengthen it. The effect depends on how the external reward is framed. Recognition that validates intrinsic motivation strengthens it. Payment that feels transactional can weaken it.

### 4.2 What Stops People from Contributing

From Robinson & DeShano (2011, Journalism Studies) and subsequent research:

1. **Time constraints** -- the most cited barrier
2. **Intimidation over journalistic standards** -- "I'm not a real journalist"
3. **Lack of perceived legitimacy** -- their contributions won't be taken seriously
4. **Low interest in creating informative content** -- people prefer sharing personal/entertainment content over news
5. **Lack of professional resources** -- no access to officials, documents, or sources
6. **Fear of getting facts wrong** -- and being publicly corrected
7. **Professional journalist skepticism** -- journalists view citizen contributions as peripheral and non-comparable to their own work, which discourages contributors

**Key insight:** Citizens were MORE interested in creating content about popular culture or daily life than journalistic content. The barrier is not just skill -- it is that "news writing" feels like a professional activity, not a natural human activity.

### 4.3 What Makes Contributors KEEP Contributing

From research on online community contributors (PMC, multiple studies):

- **Recognition that their work matters** -- volunteers who feel valued show significantly higher retention
- **Seeing tangible impact** -- their contribution led to a change, a response, or community action
- **Social bonds** -- they made friends through contributing
- **Routine and habit** -- contributing becomes part of their identity
- **Skill development** -- they got better at something through contributing
- **Community belonging** -- recognition fosters belonging, belonging drives continued engagement

### 4.4 Successful Community Reporting Models

**City Bureau Documenters (Chicago, now 19+ cities):**
- Trains and pays community members to attend and document public meetings
- 4,000+ people trained, $1 million distributed toward government accountability
- 1,400+ meetings documented in Cleveland alone (as of Oct 2025)
- 700+ published pieces informed by Documenter coverage
- Model: paid ($), trained (competence), organized (platform), recognized (byline)
- Prioritizes racial equity in recruitment

**Signal Cleveland / Signal Ohio:**
- Nonprofit newsroom combining community building with local news
- Launched Nov 2022, now expanding to Akron, Columbus, Cincinnati
- Integrates Documenters program
- Community News Fellows survey residents about what they want covered

**Bristol Cable (UK, est. 2014):**
- Fully member-owned cooperative, 2,000+ members
- Workers' cooperative structure: one member, one vote
- Produces online and print journalism made by and for Bristol residents
- Engagement with communities builds legitimacy and trust

**De Correspondent (Netherlands):**
- 60,000 paid members at ~$63/year
- Raised $1.7M in crowdfunding within weeks
- Members are treated as "knowledge partners" -- involved in writing process
- Each correspondent has a weekly newsletter deepening journalist-reader bond
- Mission: shift from sensational to foundational coverage
- Ad-free, member-funded only

**Patch (U.S.):**
- 85 full-time reporters, 3 million newsletter subscribers
- Expanded from 1,100 communities to 30,000 using AI-powered newsletters
- AI scrapes local news sites, social media, official town websites; uses LLMs to select/summarize most relevant content
- Daily/twice-weekly email with 5 articles + events list
- Community platform for user-posted questions, tips, and columns

## 5. NEWS CONSUMPTION PATTERNS

### 5.1 How People Find Local News (Pew Research, 2024)

Sources used "often or sometimes" for local news:
1. Friends, family, neighbors: **73%**
2. Local TV stations: **64%**
3. Online forums (Facebook groups, Nextdoor): **52%** (up from 38% in 2018)
4. Local newspapers: declining but still significant
5. Local radio: roughly on par with online forums
6. Online-only sources: **33%** (up from 15% in 2018)

For local political news specifically:
- Friends/family/neighbors: **70%**
- Local news outlets: **66%**
- Social media: **54%**
- Local government websites: **32%**
- Local nonprofits/advocacy groups: **31%**
- Local politicians: **30%**

### 5.2 Mobile vs. Desktop

**Mobile dominates.** Mobile traffic consistently outpaces desktop for news across the board in 2024-2025.

**Engagement varies by path:**
- Users arriving from an internal link within a news site: **148 seconds** average time on article
- Users arriving from social media: **111 seconds** -- 25% shorter
- Mobile apps attract smaller but more engaged audiences
- Mobile browsers attract larger but less attentive audiences

**Implication:** The referral source matters more than the device. Social media drives traffic but not deep reading. Direct/newsletter traffic drives real engagement.

### 5.3 Newsletter Culture for Local News

**Newsletter growth is real:**
- Paid newsletter subscriptions generated $19M in 2025 vs. $8M in 2024 (138% jump)
- Free newsletters dominate: 90%+ of new newsletters are free
- Substack: 40,000+ paying creators, 41% market share

**Local news newsletter success stories:**
- Portland Press Herald: 33% subscriber growth in 2024, 51.5% open rate
- Baltimore Banner (nonprofit): 40.6% open rate, 6,743 subscription starts (44% YoY increase)
- Press Democrat: newsletter referral traffic jumped 62% YoY with "newsletter-first" editorial approach

**Why newsletters work for local:** Direct relationship, no algorithm mediation, habit-forming (arrives at same time daily), feels personal, high open rates compared to social media reach.

### 5.4 News Avoidance

**40% of people** sometimes or often avoid the news -- joint highest ever recorded (Reuters Institute 2024 and 2025). Up from 29% in 2017. In the UK, avoidance nearly doubled from 24% (2017) to 46%.

**Two types of avoiders:**
1. Consistent avoiders: low education, little interest in news
2. Selective avoiders: struggle with news overload, protect mental wellbeing

**Reasons:** Fatigue, repetitiveness, feeling depressed/overwhelmed, low trust. Two in five UK respondents feel "worn out" by news.

**Implication for local news:** Local news may be partially insulated from avoidance because it is practical (weather, events, schools) rather than anxiety-inducing (national politics, global crises). But paywalls push people away toward free alternatives.

## 6. THE NEWS DESERT PROBLEM AND COMMUNITY RESPONSES

### 6.1 Scale of the Problem (Northwestern Medill, Oct 2025)

- **136 newspaper closures in 2025** alone (more than 2 per week)
- **213 news desert counties** in the U.S. (up from 206 previous year)
- **1,524 additional counties** with only one remaining news source
- **~50 million Americans** have limited to no access to local news
- **~3,500 newspapers lost** over the past two decades
- **270,000+ newspaper jobs lost**
- Total newspapers: down from 7,325 (2005) to 4,490 (2025)
- Web traffic to top 100 newspapers: **down 45%+ in four years**

### 6.2 What Fills the Gap

**The honest answer: mostly nothing systematic.**

Research shows that social media can help news desert residents "feel informed" but "feelings do not translate to being informed" (community resilience research, Tandfonline 2025).

**What partially fills the gap:**
- Informal community networks on Facebook/Nextdoor/WhatsApp
- 300+ digital-only news startups in last 5 years (80% digital-only)
- Nonprofit newsrooms (Signal Cleveland, Baltimore Banner, etc.)
- Regional collaborations (Gulf State Newsroom)
- AI-powered aggregation (Patch's expansion to 30K communities)
- Community newsletters and Substack-style publications

**Cooperative and community-owned models in Europe:**
- Bristol Cable (UK): member-owned cooperative
- TAZ/Panter Stiftung (Germany): cooperative newspaper, raised EUR 9.2M (2008-2024), 80% from donations
- New Internationalist: combined worker and reader membership
- EU Journalism Partnerships: disbursed a record EUR 5.26M in 2025 (40% increase over 2024)

### 6.3 The Accountability Gap

When no one covers local government:
- Municipal borrowing costs increase (Notre Dame 2018)
- Voter turnout decreases
- Political polarization increases locally
- Small problems escalate unchecked
- Community identity fragments

## 7. FINNISH / NORDIC SPECIFIC

### 7.1 Finnish News Consumption (Reuters Institute 2025)

- **67% trust in news** -- among the highest in the world (global average: 40%)
- **75% of Finns** trust most of the news they follow
- **~90% of Finns** accessed some type of local information in the last week
- In Finland, more consumption happens on publisher sites than via all third-party platforms combined
- Finland has invested heavily in native video players (both commercial and Yle)
- **89% of Finns use WhatsApp** (2022 survey)
- Facebook: 2.8 million users in Finland
- Instagram: 1.5 million users
- 82-85% of 16-34 year olds follow social media daily

### 7.2 Finnish Local Newspaper Landscape

- **~180 local papers** appearing 1-4 times per week
- Total newspapers: **176** (down ~15% since 2008)
- Dailies: **40** (down 20%+ since 2008)
- Consolidation into **50-70 strong regional brands**
- Two companies (Keskisuomalainen and Sanoma) account for over half of newspaper subscription and advertising revenues
- **Hard paywalls dominate:** Almost no news can be read without active subscription
- This drives readers to free alternatives, especially Yle regional news
- Subscriber base is mostly elderly/aging -- unlikely to shift to digital-only subscriptions

### 7.3 Yle Regional Coverage

- **31 regional news offices** across Finland: 25 Finnish-speaking, 8 Swedish-speaking, 2 Sami
- **94% reach** of the Finnish population (75% among under-15s)
- January 2025: Yle announced **309 job cuts** (10% of workforce) due to EUR 27M budget reduction
- Parliamentary working group recommended EUR 66M annual cut by 2027
- Yle's free regional news fills the gap created by paywalled local papers -- but its own funding is now threatened

### 7.4 Finnish Civic Participation Culture

- Finland has high social capital and life satisfaction
- **Volunteering peaks among 65-74 year olds** (highest odds ratio of any age group)
- Finnish voluntary sector ethos: everyone can volunteer regardless of experience, skills, or background -- no minimum requirements
- **Paradox:** Finnish teenagers have excellent civic competences but lack interest in active participation -- "happy with living in a steady representative democracy with functional safety networks"
- Nordic pupils display passivity toward direct political participation, especially social-movement participation
- **Implication:** Finns will contribute if the barrier is low and the system is trustworthy, but will not seek out contribution opportunities actively. The system must come to them.

### 7.5 Messaging Platforms in Finland

- **WhatsApp:** 89% penetration, dominant messaging platform, used heavily for family/community/school groups
- **Facebook:** 2.8M users, still primary social platform, local groups active
- **Telegram:** 1,851 communities, 14.9M total subscriber count, but content skews toward crypto/jobs/general discussion rather than local news
- **Instagram:** 1.5M users, younger demographics
- Finns consume news on publisher websites more than platforms -- a distinctive Nordic pattern

## 8. KEY SYNTHESIS: WHAT THE DATA SAYS ABOUT WHAT PEOPLE ACTUALLY WANT

### The 7 Things People Want from Local News

Based on cross-referencing all the above data:

1. **Weather and immediate practical information** (90% consumption rate). Not "journalism" in the traditional sense. Functional, daily, habitual.

2. **Safety and crime information** (77%). Both specific incidents ("what happened on my street") and patterns ("is my area getting safer or more dangerous").

3. **Accountability coverage they can trust** (85% say local news is important for community wellbeing). They want someone watching local government, schools, police. They mostly will not pay for it.

4. **Community connection** -- births, deaths, events, local achievements, human stories. This is what people miss most when papers close. It is the connective tissue of community identity.

5. **Events and activities** (platforms now dominate this). What is happening near me this weekend. Where can I take my kids. What is opening/closing.

6. **Recommendations and services** (platforms dominate here too). Plumbers, restaurants, childcare. Nextdoor and Facebook groups have captured this entirely.

7. **A voice and a sense of being heard.** The Nextdoor trust data is telling: people trust neighbors (36%) more than local news (10%). They want to participate in the information ecosystem, not just consume it. But they need low-friction ways to do so.

### The Central Tensions

**Trust vs. payment:** 74% trust local news, 16% pay for it.

**Want to contribute vs. barriers:** People want to share what they know, but writing feels like a professional skill, and they fear judgment.

**News avoidance vs. information need:** 40% avoid news, but 90% consume weather and 77% consume crime news. People avoid "news" as a category (depressing, overwhelming) but hunger for local practical information.

**Platforms vs. publishers:** Platforms have captured community information (events, services, buying/selling). Publishers retain advantage only in accountability journalism and breaking news. The comprehensive "community hub" role of the local paper is gone.

**Finland's specific tension:** High trust + hard paywalls + aging subscriber base + Yle cuts = a system that works for now but is structurally fragile. Young Finns are not entering the subscriber pipeline, and free alternatives are being defunded.
