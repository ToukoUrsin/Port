# Journalism Craft Reference

Research compiled 2026-03-07 for building an AI pipeline that turns voice memos into proper journalism articles. Rules, structures, and techniques that make professional news writing different from regular writing.

---

## 1. Article Structures

There are four primary structures used in professional journalism. Each serves a different purpose. The choice depends on story type, urgency, and how much narrative the material supports.

### 1A. Inverted Pyramid

The workhorse of news journalism. Most important information first, then supporting details in descending order of importance.

```
MOST IMPORTANT FACT (the lead)
  ESSENTIAL DETAILS (who, what, when, where)
    SUPPORTING INFORMATION (how, why, context)
      BACKGROUND / LESS CRITICAL DETAILS
        LEAST ESSENTIAL INFO (can be cut from bottom)
```

**When to use:** Breaking news, time-sensitive stories, hard news, any story where the reader needs the point immediately. Default structure for local news.

**Strengths:** Readers get the point even if they stop after one paragraph. Editors can cut from the bottom without losing essential meaning. Works well for digital/mobile where attention spans are short.

**Weaknesses:** No narrative arc. Not engaging for complex human-interest stories. Can feel mechanical.

**Example:**
> A 6.2 magnitude earthquake struck central Idaho early Tuesday morning, damaging at least 40 buildings in the town of Stanley and injuring three residents, according to the U.S. Geological Survey.

### 1B. Hourglass

Starts like an inverted pyramid (summary lead + key facts), then pivots into a chronological narrative. Three distinct parts.

```
THE TOP (4-6 paragraphs)
  Summary lead
  Most pressing facts answered
  Reader is informed even if they stop here

THE TURN (1 sentence or phrase)
  Transitional phrase signaling the shift
  "According to witnesses..."
  "Police described what happened next..."

THE NARRATIVE (chronological storytelling)
  Beginning, middle, end
  Vivid details, dialogue, background
  Shows what the top merely told
```

**When to use:** Crime reports, court cases, dramatic events, any story where both urgency AND narrative context matter. Works for meetings where something dramatic happened.

**The turn is the critical piece.** It bridges summary and narrative. Common turn phrases:
- "According to police, events unfolded this way:"
- "Witnesses described the scene:"
- "The trouble began around 3 p.m."

**Example turn:** "Officers who responded to the call described what they found:" -- then shift into chronological account.

### 1C. Diamond Structure

Opens with an anecdote, broadens to the general issue, then narrows back to the specific story.

```
SPECIFIC ANECDOTE (one person, one scene)
  NUT GRAF (here's what this is really about)
    BROADER CONTEXT (data, other examples, expert views)
      DETAILED REPORTING (the middle bulk of the story)
    NARROWING BACK
  RETURN TO OPENING ANECDOTE
RESOLUTION / KICKER
```

**When to use:** Feature stories, human-interest pieces, opinion journalism. Stories where a single person's experience illustrates a larger trend.

**Key rule:** The opening anecdote must genuinely represent the broader issue. Cherry-picking an unrepresentative case is editorializing.

### 1D. Narrative / Chronological

Events told in the order they happened. Uses scene-setting, character, conflict, and resolution like a story.

**When to use:** Long-form investigative pieces, feature writing, magazine journalism. Rarely appropriate for daily local news unless the story is inherently dramatic and the publication allows the space.

**Weaknesses:** Requires reader commitment. Buries the most important fact. Not suitable for breaking news.

### Which Structure for Local News?

**Default: Inverted pyramid.** Most local news stories -- council votes, crime reports, business openings, school events -- should use this.

**When to upgrade to hourglass:** The story has a dramatic chronological sequence (a rescue, a confrontation at a meeting, a court proceeding).

**When to use diamond:** Longer features about community trends, profiles of local figures, stories where one person's experience illustrates a neighborhood-wide issue.

**Rule of thumb:** If the story can be told in under 500 words, use inverted pyramid. If it needs 800+, consider hourglass or diamond.

## 2. The Lead (Lede)

The most important sentence in any news article. Gets the reader in or loses them.

### 2A. Summary Lead (Hard News Default)

One sentence. Covers the most important facts. Maximum 25 words (strict guideline from journalism educators). Addresses at minimum 3 of the 5 W's (Who, What, When, Where, Why) plus How.

**Formula:**
```
[WHO] [DID WHAT] [WHEN], [WHERE], [according to/WHY context].
```

**Good example:**
> The San Marcos City Council voted 4-1 Tuesday to approve a $2.3 million renovation of the downtown library.

**Bad example (too long, too much):**
> At Tuesday night's regularly scheduled meeting of the San Marcos City Council, which was held at City Hall in the council chambers, members voted by a margin of 4-1 to approve the renovation project for the downtown library that will cost $2.3 million and is expected to take 18 months.

**Rules for summary leads:**
- One sentence, maximum two
- 25 words is the target (some say 35 max)
- Must be a complete, fluid sentence -- not a headline
- Lead with the news, not the source ("A new stadium will be built" not "The mayor announced that a new stadium will be built")
- Use active voice
- Strongest verb you can find

### 2B. Delayed-Identification Lead

Names the person's role/description first, reveals their name in paragraph two. Used when the person is not famous and their situation is more newsworthy than their identity.

**Example:**
> A 34-year-old Escondido mother of three was killed Saturday when her car struck a median on Interstate 15, authorities said.
>
> Maria Torres was driving northbound near the Deer Springs Road exit around 2:15 a.m. when the crash occurred, according to the California Highway Patrol.

**When to use:** When the person is not a public figure. The reader cares about what happened before who it happened to.

### 2C. Anecdotal / Narrative Lead

Opens with a 3-5 paragraph mini-story that illustrates the larger theme. Always followed by a nut graf.

**Example:**
> When James Porter opened his front door last Thursday, the water was already ankle-deep on his porch. By the time he carried his dog to the truck, it was at his knees.
>
> Porter is one of 47 residents in the Willow Creek neighborhood who have been flooded three times in the past two years -- a problem city engineers say stems from a drainage system built in 1968 that was never designed to handle modern development.

**Rules:**
- The anecdote must represent the broader story (not an outlier)
- Must be followed immediately by a nut graf that zooms out
- Keep it to 3-5 paragraphs max before hitting the nut graf
- Use specific, concrete details (ankle-deep, not "significant flooding")

### 2D. Scene-Setting Lead

Uses sensory details to place the reader in a location. Works for features about places, events, or atmosphere.

**Example:**
> The smell of fresh tortillas drifts across Main Street from the open kitchen window of Rosa's Cafe, where a line already stretches to the sidewalk at 7 a.m.

### Leads to AVOID (Common Amateur Mistakes)

**The "Duh" lead** -- states something obvious:
> BAD: "The City Council held a meeting Monday night."
> (Of course they did. What happened AT the meeting?)

**The question lead** -- asks the reader a question:
> BAD: "Have you ever wondered what happens to your recycling?"
> (Don't quiz your reader. Tell them.)

**The quote lead** -- opens with a direct quotation:
> BAD: "We're going to fight this all the way," said Mayor Garcia.
> (Confusing without context. Who? Fighting what?)

**The attribution lead** -- leads with the source instead of the news:
> BAD: "Mayor Garcia told reporters Tuesday that the city plans to build a new fire station."
> GOOD: "The city plans to build a new fire station on Oak Avenue, Mayor Garcia announced Tuesday."

**The "yesterday" lead** -- focuses on old information:
> BAD: "A jury began deliberating Monday in the murder trial of..."
> GOOD: "A jury is expected to reach a verdict today in the murder trial of..." (if writing Tuesday)

**The double-negative lead** -- reports what DIDN'T happen:
> BAD: "Sixty percent of residents did not vote in Tuesday's election."
> BETTER: "Voter turnout reached 40 percent in Tuesday's election, the lowest in a decade."

**The fanciful lead** -- exaggerates or misleads to create drama:
> BAD: "The school board dropped a bombshell Tuesday night."
> (What bombshell? And "bombshell" is editorializing.)

## 3. The Nut Graf

The most important paragraph after the lead. Answers the question: "So what? Why should I care? Why are you telling me this NOW?"

### Definition

Short for "nutshell paragraph." A paragraph that tells the reader what the whole story is about and why they should read it. It provides the context, the stakes, and the timeliness.

### Placement

- In an **inverted pyramid** with a summary lead: the nut graf is essentially baked into the lead itself (the lead IS the nutshell).
- In a **feature/anecdotal lead**: the nut graf appears in paragraph 3-5, right after the opening anecdote ends. It's the paragraph where the writer steps back from the specific and reveals the general.
- **Never later than paragraph 6.** If the reader has to read six paragraphs without knowing why they're reading, they'll leave.

### What It Must Do (Four Functions)

1. **Justify the story** -- explain why the reader should care
2. **Provide transition** -- connect the anecdotal lead to the broader story
3. **Explain timeliness** -- why NOW? What changed?
4. **Include supporting evidence** -- a number, a fact, something concrete that demonstrates importance

### Example

After an anecdotal lead about one resident's flooding experience:

> Porter is one of 47 residents in the Willow Creek subdivision who have reported flood damage since January 2024. The neighborhood sits at the lowest point of a drainage system built in 1968 -- before the Riverside Estates development upstream added 200 homes and 14 acres of impervious surface. The city has allocated $1.8 million for a fix, but construction won't begin until next spring.

This nut graf does all four things: justifies (47 affected residents), transitions (from Porter to the neighborhood), explains timeliness (problem ongoing, fix delayed), and provides supporting evidence ($1.8M, 200 homes, 14 acres).

### Common Nut Graf Mistakes

- **Too vague:** "Flooding has been a problem in the area." (What area? How much? Since when?)
- **Too long:** More than 2-3 paragraphs and you've lost momentum.
- **Reveals too much:** If you give away the whole story in the nut graf, there's no reason to keep reading.
- **Missing entirely:** The reader finishes the anecdote and has no idea why they read it.

## 4. Quote Handling

How professional journalists select, trim, attribute, and place quotes. This is one of the areas where amateur writing diverges most sharply from professional practice.

### 4A. When to Quote Directly

**Quote directly when the source says something that:**
- Expresses emotion, opinion, or personal experience
- Uses distinctive, memorable, or authoritative language
- Would lose impact if paraphrased
- Is a public official making a significant statement

**Paraphrase when the source is:**
- Stating dry facts ("The meeting starts at 7 p.m.")
- Providing background information
- Speaking in long, winding sentences that need tightening
- Saying something you can state more clearly

**Rule of thumb:** Paraphrase facts. Quote feelings, opinions, and distinctive language.

### 4B. Quote Selection and Trimming

- You can remove filler words ("um," "uh," "you know," "like") without notation
- You can fix minor grammatical errors only if leaving them would embarrass the source AND the meaning is clear
- You CANNOT change the meaning, make someone sound smarter or dumber, or alter the substance
- **Patching:** You can combine sentences from different parts of the same interview into one quote, as long as the meaning isn't altered. This is standard practice.
- Ideal direct quote length: 1-3 sentences. Longer quotes should be broken up with attribution or paraphrase.

### 4C. Quote Placement in the Story

**The LQTQ pattern** (Lead-Quote-Transition-Quote):
```
[Paraphrased context / setup]
"Direct quote from source," Source Name said.
[Transition paragraph: new fact, context, or setup for next quote]
"Second direct quote," Source Name said.
```

**Rules:**
- Never stack two quotes from different sources back-to-back without a transition paragraph between them
- Include a direct quote every 3-4 paragraphs to keep the story grounded in real voices
- For multi-sentence quotes, insert attribution after the first sentence, not at the end:

```
GOOD: "We've been dealing with this for two years," Garcia said. "At some point the city has to step up."

BAD: "We've been dealing with this for two years. At some point the city has to step up," Garcia said.
```

- **Kicker quote:** End the story with a strong quote that encapsulates the theme or looks forward. But don't overuse this -- it's a cliche. Sometimes a narrative ending or a "circle back to the lead" ending is stronger.

### 4D. Partial Quotes

Use sparingly. A partial quote embeds a few of the source's words inside a paraphrased sentence.

**Example:**
> Garcia called the proposal "a band-aid on a bullet wound" and said she would vote against it.

**When to use:** When one phrase is distinctive but the surrounding sentence isn't worth quoting in full.

**When NOT to use:** Don't use partial quotes for ordinary language. ("She said the plan was 'good'" -- why are you quoting "good"?)

### 4E. What to Never Do with Quotes

- Never fabricate a quote
- Never take a quote out of context to change its meaning
- Never use a quote to state something YOU want to say (ventriloquism)
- Never publish partial quotes that alter how the full quote would be perceived
- Never quote someone without identifying them (except in rare source-protection situations, which have their own rules)

## 5. Attribution Rules

### 5A. The Verb: "Said" is King

**"Said" is the only attribution verb you need 90% of the time.**

Data from analysis of BBC, Daily Mail, and BuzzFeed articles: "said" accounts for ~65% of all speech attributions. "Told" is ~11%. "Added" is ~10%. These three cover almost 90% of all reported speech in professional journalism.

**Why "said" works:**
- It's invisible. Readers don't notice it. It doesn't interrupt the flow.
- It's neutral. It carries no judgment about whether the statement is true, false, important, or trivial.
- It keeps the journalist out of the story. Alternatives inject the reporter's interpretation.

**The problem with alternatives:**

| Verb | Problem |
|------|---------|
| "claimed" | Implies the reporter doubts the statement is true |
| "admitted" | Implies guilt or reluctance |
| "revealed" | Implies the information was hidden |
| "noted" | Implies the reporter agrees the statement is noteworthy or correct |
| "explained" | Implies the statement needed explaining (sometimes fine, often editorial) |
| "stated" | Sounds bureaucratic and stiff |
| "declared" | Sounds grandiose |
| "asserted" | Implies aggression or defensiveness |

**Acceptable alternatives (used sparingly):**
- "told" -- when emphasizing who was told ("told the council," "told reporters")
- "added" -- when a source continues after a break in quoting
- "according to" -- for documents, reports, written statements
- "wrote" -- for written communication
- "asked" -- for questions

### 5B. First Reference vs. Subsequent Reference

**First mention:** Full name + title/role.
> San Marcos Fire Chief David Chen said the department responded within four minutes.

**All subsequent mentions:** Last name only. No courtesy titles (Mr., Mrs., Ms.) unless needed to distinguish people with the same last name.
> Chen said the fire started in the kitchen.

**Never:**
> Mr. Chen said...  (AP style drops courtesy titles)
> David said...  (too informal for news)
> The fire chief said... (acceptable occasionally for variety, but name is preferred)

### 5C. Attribution Placement

**For a single-sentence quote:**
> "The bridge will be closed for six weeks," Chen said.

**For a multi-sentence quote, attribute after the first sentence:**
> "The bridge will be closed for six weeks," Chen said. "We expect significant traffic delays on Route 78 during that time."

**When NOT to lead with attribution:**
> BAD: Chen said, "The bridge will be closed for six weeks."
> GOOD: "The bridge will be closed for six weeks," Chen said.

(Exception: When a long explanatory clause follows the attribution, inversion is acceptable: "Chen said that the closure, which was originally planned for August, had been moved up because of...")

### 5D. Attributing Claims Without Taking Sides

When two sides disagree, attribute both positions and let the reader judge:

> Garcia said the project would bring 200 jobs to the city. Opponents, including the Riverside Homeowners Association, said the development would increase traffic and lower property values.

**Do not write:**
> Garcia claimed the project would bring 200 jobs (implies doubt)
> Garcia correctly noted that the project would bring 200 jobs (takes her side)

**When facts are verifiable, state them as facts:**
> The project is zoned for 150 residential units, according to city planning documents.

You don't need to attribute established facts to anyone. The sky is blue. The city's population is 42,000. The meeting lasted three hours. These are facts, not claims.

## 6. Objectivity and Tone

### 6A. The Core Rule

Energy should come from the facts themselves, not from the journalist's language. If the facts are dramatic, the writing should be plain. The facts do the work.

**Example of good neutral writing that's still powerful:**
> The president today pardoned a staff member convicted of lying to a jury about the vice president's participation in a campaign against an FBI agent's reputation.

That sentence is entirely factual. It contains no adjectives. It's devastating because of what it says, not how it says it.

### 6B. Words and Phrases That Editorialize

These inject opinion into news copy. An AI pipeline must strip or flag them:

**Judgment adjectives/adverbs:**
- "controversial" (who says it's controversial? Attribute it.)
- "shocking," "stunning," "dramatic"
- "interesting," "impressive," "remarkable"
- "tragic," "devastating" (describe what happened; let the reader decide if it's tragic)
- "avoidable," "unnecessary"
- "only" (as in "only $500 was allocated" -- "only" is a judgment)
- "finally" (implies they should have done it sooner)
- "unfortunately," "sadly," "thankfully"

**Opinion-indicating language:**
- "I think," "I believe," "in my opinion" (never in news copy)
- "It is clear that..." (clear to whom?)
- "Obviously..." (if it's obvious, you don't need to say it)
- "Critics/supporters say..." without naming them (who specifically?)

**Sensationalist language:**
- "bombshell," "firestorm," "backlash," "slammed" (as in "slammed the proposal")
- "skyrocketed," "plummeted" (use actual numbers)
- "crisis" (unless it is officially designated a crisis)

**The first/second person test:** News copy should contain zero instances of I, me, my, we, our, you, your -- unless inside a direct quote from a source.

### 6C. News vs. Editorial: The Bright Line

| Element | News Article | Editorial / Opinion |
|---------|-------------|-------------------|
| Purpose | Inform | Persuade |
| Voice | Third person, neutral | First person or institutional "we" |
| Opinion | Attributed to sources only | Author's own opinion |
| Adjectives | Minimal, factual | Freely used |
| Conclusion | None stated | Argument leads to recommendation |
| Labeling | "News" or no label | "Opinion," "Editorial," "Commentary" |

**Grey area: "News Analysis"** -- explores causes and consequences beyond basic facts. Should be labeled explicitly. An AI pipeline should NOT produce analysis unless the output is labeled as such.

### 6D. Stripping Editorializing from Raw Community Input

When converting voice memos or community-submitted content into news articles, these transformations are needed:

| Raw Input | Professional Version |
|-----------|---------------------|
| "The school board made a terrible decision" | "The school board voted 5-2 to close Jefferson Elementary" |
| "Everyone is upset about this" | "Twelve residents spoke against the proposal during public comment" |
| "The mayor finally did something about it" | "The mayor announced the program Tuesday" |
| "It's obvious the developer doesn't care about the neighborhood" | "Several residents said they felt the developer had not addressed their concerns" |
| "Crime is out of control" | "Police reported 47 burglaries in the district this quarter, up from 31 in the same period last year" |
| "The new park is beautiful" | "The park features a 0.5-mile walking trail, a playground, and 40 newly planted oak trees" |

**Pattern:** Replace subjective judgments with specific, verifiable facts. Replace vague crowd claims ("everyone," "people are saying") with named sources or specific counts.

## 7. Local News Conventions

What makes local news writing different from national news.

### 7A. Geographic Specificity

Local news names exact streets, intersections, neighborhoods, and landmarks. National news says "a suburb of Chicago." Local news says "the 400 block of Elm Street in the Riverside neighborhood."

**Examples:**
- NATIONAL: "A fire at a warehouse in Houston..."
- LOCAL: "A fire at the former McKinley Grain warehouse at 742 Industrial Boulevard..."

Readers know these places. Naming them creates connection and lets affected residents know whether the story is in their area.

### 7B. Name Every Source

Local news uses full names for everyone because the community knows these people. National news might say "a school spokesperson." Local news says "district spokesperson Maria Reyes."

In local news, the person quoted IS part of the news value. Readers may know them personally.

### 7C. Impact Framing

Every local story must answer: **"What does this mean for the people who live here?"**

- A budget vote is not about numbers. It's about which programs get cut.
- A zoning change is not about land use categories. It's about what gets built next to your house.
- A road closure is not about infrastructure. It's about your commute.

**The "your commute" test:** Can you explain why this story matters to someone eating breakfast and deciding whether to keep reading?

### 7D. Community Tone Without Boosterism

Local news serves the community but does not cheerlead for it. Coverage of a new business opening is factual, not promotional. Coverage of a community event includes attendance numbers and quotes, not "what a wonderful event."

**Boosting (bad):** "The annual Fall Festival drew a fantastic crowd and once again proved why our town is such a great place to live."

**Neutral (good):** "About 3,000 people attended Saturday's Fall Festival at Veterans Park, up from 2,200 last year, organizers said."

### 7E. Council/Meeting Coverage Convention

Local news spends significant space on government meetings. The convention:

1. Lead with the most newsworthy vote or decision
2. Include the vote count (4-1, unanimous, etc.)
3. Name who voted how on controversial items
4. Include quotes from both sides
5. Include any public comment highlights
6. Mention other business briefly at the end

**Example lead:**
> The Millville Board of Education voted 6-3 Tuesday to eliminate the district's middle school arts program, a cut officials said would save $340,000 but that parents called "devastating."

## 8. Photo Journalism Basics

### 8A. Caption Writing: AP Style Convention

Captions follow a strict two-sentence format:

**Sentence 1 (PRESENT TENSE):** Describes who is in the photo, what they are doing, where, and when. This sentence uses present tense to create immediacy.

**Sentence 2 (PAST TENSE):** Provides context -- why the photo is significant, what the broader news story is.

**Example:**
> San Marcos Fire Chief David Chen addresses residents at a community meeting at Jefferson Elementary School on Tuesday, March 4, 2026. The meeting was called after three house fires in the Riverside neighborhood prompted calls for additional hydrant inspections.

### 8B. Caption Rules

**Do:**
- Identify every recognizable person by full name
- For multiple people, identify left to right: "(from left) Maria Torres, James Porter and David Chen"
- Include city/state (or country if outside the U.S.)
- Include the date; include day of the week if within the past two weeks
- Write in complete sentences

**Do not:**
- Use "looks on," "poses," "is pictured," "is shown" -- these are filler
- State the visually obvious: don't write "smiles" if the person is clearly smiling
- Editorialize: don't write "an angry voter" -- describe what you can observe ("a resident who spoke against the proposal")
- Use descriptive adjectives like "beautiful," "dramatic," "horrifying" -- the photo should convey that
- Assume emotions: "happy," "sad," "frustrated" -- unless the person told you

**File photos:** Must be labeled "FILE" or "file photo" in the caption. Include original date.

**Photo illustrations and manipulated images:** Must disclose manipulation in the caption or credit line. Beyond cropping, sizing, and color correction, any alteration must be noted.

### 8C. Photo-to-Story Integration

- Every photo should directly relate to the story content
- Captions should add information not already in the story, or at minimum connect the visual to a specific part of the story
- A caption is not a description of what's visible -- it's a micro-story that contextualizes the image

## 9. Missing Context / Gaps

How professional editors identify what's missing from a story.

### 9A. The Editor's Question Set

Before a story is published, editors (or an AI acting as editor) should ask:

**Completeness:**
1. Does the story answer all five W's and H? Which ones are missing, and is there a good reason?
2. Does the reader have enough context to understand the story without prior knowledge?
3. Are all stakeholders represented? Who is affected but not quoted?
4. Is there a "the other side" that hasn't been contacted?
5. Did the reporter reach out multiple times, in multiple ways, to people potentially harmed by the content?

**Accuracy:**
6. Are all names spelled correctly? Verified against official sources?
7. Are all numbers, dates, and ages correct? Has the math been checked?
8. Are all titles and roles accurate and current?
9. Are superlatives ("the first," "the largest," "the oldest") sourced and verified?
10. Do all quotes accurately represent what the source said?

**Fairness:**
11. Does the story give the right of response to anyone accused or criticized?
12. Are there unnamed sources? Is their anonymity justified?
13. Could a reasonable reader from any side feel the story was fair?

**Structure:**
14. Does the story have a clear lead that delivers the news?
15. Is there a nut graf that explains why this matters?
16. Does the story leave any important questions unanswered?
17. Is every paragraph doing work? Can any be cut without losing meaning?

**Source quality:**
18. Are there at least three credible firsthand sources, including at least two human sources?
19. Have official documents or records been consulted where available?
20. Are all claims from one source corroborated by at least one other?

### 9B. The "What's Missing" Checklist

Common gaps that professional editors catch:

| Gap | Question to Ask |
|-----|----------------|
| Missing numbers | "How many? How much? What percentage?" |
| Missing timeline | "When did this start? What happens next? What's the deadline?" |
| Missing opposition | "Who disagrees? Have they been contacted?" |
| Missing impact | "Who is affected? How many people? What changes for them?" |
| Missing history | "Has this happened before? What was the outcome?" |
| Missing comparison | "How does this compare to other places / previous years?" |
| Missing next steps | "What happens now? When is the next decision point?" |
| Missing definition | "Will the reader understand this term / acronym / process?" |
| Unattributed claim | "Who says this? Is it a fact or someone's opinion?" |
| Single source | "Is there only one voice in this story?" |

### 9C. The Read-Aloud Test

Professional editors read stories aloud (or have someone else read them). This catches:
- Awkward phrasing that looks fine on screen
- Missing transitions between paragraphs
- Quotes that don't sound like natural speech
- Run-on sentences
- Unintended tone or emphasis

### 9D. The Backwards Spelling Check

Read the story backwards, word by word, checking each word's spelling individually. This prevents your brain from auto-correcting errors because it removes the context that lets you skip over typos.

## 10. Common Amateur Mistakes

These are the specific errors that citizen journalists, community contributors, and first-year journalism students consistently make. These are the things an AI pipeline must detect and correct.

### 10A. Structural Mistakes

**1. Burying the lead.**
The most important information is in paragraph 3 or 4 instead of paragraph 1. The writer "warms up" before getting to the point.
> AMATEUR: "Last Tuesday, the City Council gathered for their monthly meeting. Several items were on the agenda. After much discussion, they voted to close the community pool."
> PROFESSIONAL: "The City Council voted Tuesday to close the Riverside community pool, citing $400,000 in needed repairs the city cannot afford."

**2. No nut graf.**
The reader finishes the opening and has no idea why they should care or what the story is about.

**3. No structure at all.**
The story jumps from idea to idea without logical flow. Information about the same topic appears in multiple disconnected places.

**4. Story is too long.**
Amateur writers include everything they know. Professional writers include only what the reader needs. If a paragraph doesn't serve the story, cut it.

### 10B. Voice and Tone Mistakes

**5. Writing in first person.**
> AMATEUR: "I attended the meeting and was surprised by the decision."
> PROFESSIONAL: "The board voted 5-2 to approve the rezoning, drawing objections from three residents who spoke during public comment."

**6. Writing in second person.**
> AMATEUR: "You won't believe what happened at the school board meeting."
> PROFESSIONAL: "The school board voted Tuesday to eliminate the middle school arts program."

**7. Editorializing.**
> AMATEUR: "In a shocking move, the council rejected the popular proposal."
> PROFESSIONAL: "The council voted 3-2 to reject the proposal, which had drawn support from more than 200 residents who signed a petition."

**8. Cheerleading / boosterism.**
> AMATEUR: "The wonderful new community garden is a testament to our neighborhood's spirit."
> PROFESSIONAL: "The community garden at 200 Pine St. opened Saturday with 40 plots, all of which have been claimed by residents, organizer Maria Torres said."

**9. Using emotional language in place of facts.**
> AMATEUR: "Residents are furious about the dangerous intersection."
> PROFESSIONAL: "Seven crashes have occurred at the intersection of Oak and Main in the past 18 months, according to police records. Fifteen residents spoke at Tuesday's council meeting asking for a traffic signal."

### 10C. Quote Mistakes

**10. Quoting dry facts.**
> AMATEUR: "The meeting will be held on Tuesday at 7 p.m.," said the mayor.
> PROFESSIONAL: The meeting is scheduled for 7 p.m. Tuesday, Mayor Chen said. (Paraphrase the fact; save quotes for opinions and distinctive language.)

**11. Not attributing opinions.**
> AMATEUR: "The plan is a waste of money."
> PROFESSIONAL: "The plan is a waste of money," said resident James Porter, who organized a petition against it.

**12. Using "claimed," "admitted," or other loaded attribution verbs.**
> AMATEUR: "The developer claimed the project would create jobs."
> PROFESSIONAL: "The developer said the project would create 200 jobs."

**13. Stacking quotes without transitions.**
> AMATEUR: "This is a great day," Garcia said. "I disagree completely," Porter said.
> PROFESSIONAL: "This is a great day for Riverside," Garcia said. [transition paragraph with context] Porter, who has opposed the project since it was proposed in January, disagreed. "This is not what the neighborhood asked for," he said.

### 10D. Factual Mistakes

**14. Not verifying information.**
Publishing numbers, names, or claims from a single source without cross-checking. The rule: cross-check with at least two credible sources.

**15. Using unverified social media posts as sources.**
A Facebook post or tweet is not a source. It's a lead. You still need to confirm independently.

**16. Getting names and titles wrong.**
Misspelling names, using outdated titles, or inventing titles ("City Official" when their actual title is "Assistant Director of Public Works").

**17. Math errors.**
Getting percentages wrong. Confusing millions and billions. Not checking whether the numbers in the story add up.

### 10E. AP Style Mistakes

**18. Using the Oxford comma.**
AP style omits it. "Red, white and blue" not "red, white, and blue."

**19. Wrong number style.**
Spell out one through nine. Use digits for 10 and above. Never start a sentence with a digit (except years).

**20. Wrong date format.**
> WRONG: "March 7th, 2026" or "3/7/26"
> RIGHT: "March 7, 2026" -- no "st," "nd," "rd," or "th" after dates. Abbreviate only Jan., Feb., Aug., Sept., Oct., Nov., Dec. when used with a specific date. Spell out March, April, May, June, July when used with a date.

**21. Courtesy titles.**
AP style does not use Mr., Mrs., Ms., Miss unless needed to distinguish people with the same surname or in a direct quote.

**22. Passive voice.**
> AMATEUR: "The decision was made by the council."
> PROFESSIONAL: "The council made the decision." (Or better: "The council voted to...")

### 10F. Missing Elements

**23. No "what happens next."**
Stories end without telling the reader what's coming: the next meeting, the deadline, the expected decision date. Readers want to know what to watch for.

**24. No opposing view.**
Only one side of a controversy is represented. Every contentious story needs at minimum a "declined to comment" notation for the other side, and ideally actual quotes.

**25. No impact statement.**
The story tells you what happened but not why it matters to anyone. The "so what?" is absent.

## Quick-Reference Cheat Sheet

For AI prompt engineering -- the rules an article-generation system must enforce:

```
STRUCTURE
- Lead in paragraph 1 (not paragraph 3)
- Nut graf by paragraph 3-5 (for feature leads) or built into the lead (for summary leads)
- LQTQ rhythm: Lead/context, Quote, Transition, Quote
- End with a kicker (strong quote, circle-back, or forward-looking statement)

VOICE
- Third person only (no I, we, you)
- Past tense for events, present tense for ongoing conditions
- Active voice default
- Zero adjectives that carry judgment
- Zero sensationalist language

ATTRIBUTION
- "said" for 90% of attribution
- Full name + title on first reference
- Last name only on subsequent references
- No courtesy titles (Mr., Mrs.)
- Attribute after first sentence of multi-sentence quotes

QUOTES
- Paraphrase facts, quote opinions and emotions
- Never stack quotes without transition paragraphs
- 1-3 sentences per quote block
- Partial quotes used sparingly and only for distinctive phrases

FACTS
- Numbers: spell out one through nine, digits for 10+
- Dates: March 7, 2026 (no "th", no abbreviation for March)
- No Oxford comma
- Every claim attributed or verifiable
- Math checked

LOCAL NEWS SPECIFICS
- Name exact streets, intersections, neighborhoods
- Full names for all sources
- Impact framing: what does this mean for residents?
- Vote counts on council/board decisions
- "What happens next" in final paragraphs

PHOTO CAPTIONS
- Sentence 1: present tense, who/what/where/when
- Sentence 2: past tense, context/significance
- Identify all people by name, left to right
- No "looks on," "poses," "is pictured"
- No emotion assumptions
```
