# HOW THE DECK SHOULD FLOW AND FEEL
# Date: 2026-03-08 UTC+3

This is the creative direction — not what's on each slide, but how the whole thing FEELS as a 4-minute experience. Read this before designing anything.

---

## THE ARC IN ONE BREATH

Loss → Hope → Proof → Awe → Trust → Resolve.

The audience starts feeling the weight of something broken. Then they see a glimmer — a reframe that says "the pieces are all here." Then they watch it happen live. They feel surprise, then a deeper awe when the AI reveals what was missing. Then the economics make them believe it's real. Then the close makes them feel like they just witnessed something that should exist.

The last feeling is the one that matters: **"This should exist."**

---

## THE EMOTIONAL TEXTURE OF EACH BEAT

### Beat 1: GRAVITY [0:00 - 0:25]

The room gets quiet. This is not a fun opener. This is not "hey everyone, we're excited to present." This starts heavy.

"213 counties. Zero journalists."

Pause. Let it land.

The screen shows a number so large it fills the frame. Then a map with grey voids — places where nobody is watching. The speaker's voice is calm, almost somber. Not dramatic — factual. The facts ARE dramatic enough.

"When the newspaper closes, corruption rises 7%."

This is the moment when the audience stops thinking about their phones. The gravity is real because the problem is real. We don't need to manufacture emotion — the statistics carry genuine weight. Children in these communities grow up in places where nobody writes down what happens. That's not a pitch device. That's the truth.

The feeling: a room full of people who just realized something they never thought about. Not guilt — awareness.

**Tone:** Still. Measured. Authoritative. No excitement yet. The speaker is a person who has looked at this problem and understands its weight.

**Visual mood:** Dark. Almost black background. White text. The numbers glow slightly. The map feels like looking at the world from above and seeing the dark spots where the lights went out.

---

### Beat 2: THE TURN [0:25 - 0:50]

The mood shifts. Not from dark to light — from despair to recognition.

"But the information isn't gone."

This is the pivot. The audience just felt the weight of the problem. Now they feel a different kind of surprise: the raw material for the solution already exists. It's in people's heads, in their voice messages, in the photos they take. It's everywhere — just scattered, unstructured, ephemeral.

The visual splits: on the left, a messy Facebook post. Informal. Gets 3 likes. Disappears. On the right, the same information — but structured. A headline. Attributed quotes. A photo with a real caption. It looks like journalism because it IS journalism. The only difference: structure.

The arrow between them says "AI" — but it should feel less like technology and more like alchemy. Like something hidden was always there and just needed to be drawn out.

"Nobody turns it into stories people actually read. Until now."

The "until now" is not shouted. It's said with quiet confidence. The speaker knows what comes next. The audience doesn't yet.

**Tone:** Rising. Not excitement — recognition. Like pointing at something obvious that everyone missed.

**Visual mood:** The split screen should feel like a before/after photograph. The left side is noisy, cluttered, disposable. The right side is clean, permanent, trustworthy. Same content. Different container. The container matters.

---

### Beat 3: THE SETUP [0:50 - 1:10]

Quick. Clean. Functional. This is not where the pitch lives — this is the runway before takeoff.

Three icons. Microphone → Brain → Newspaper. "Record. Generate. Publish." Below: "5 minutes."

The speaker moves fast here because the real explanation is the demo. This slide exists so the audience has a mental model BEFORE they see it happen. Without this slide, the demo is confusing. With it, the demo is confirmation.

"Record a voice memo. Snap some photos. AI writes a real news article. A second AI pass reviews it — are the facts grounded? Whose voice is missing? You hit publish. Your town has a newspaper again."

The critical phrase is "whose voice is missing" — planted here, it pays off in slide 5. The audience hears it but doesn't fully understand it yet. That's intentional. It's a seed.

**Tone:** Brisk. Confident. Almost casual — like explaining something so obvious it barely needs explanation. The confidence signals "we've thought about this deeply enough that we can describe it simply."

**Visual mood:** Bright, clean, minimal. The three icons should feel inevitable — of COURSE this is how it works. The simplicity is the point. A phone is all you need.

---

### Beat 4: THE MOMENT [1:10 - 2:20]

This is 70 seconds. This is where everything is won or lost.

It starts with Samu's video — 20-25 seconds, sped up, lightly scored with ambient music. Samu walks the hackathon floor. He interviews a team. He takes photos. He records his observations. The video feels alive, human, casual. It's NOT a polished corporate video. It should feel like watching a friend do something interesting. The judges should recognize the venue they're sitting in.

"He spent 10 minutes."

Then the app appears on screen. The raw inputs are visible — the audio waveform, the photos, the note. It looks like raw material. Unprocessed. Human.

The speaker says: "Watch."

One word. Then they hit Generate.

The SSE events tick through on the projected screen. "Listening to your recording..." A beat. "Writing your article..." A longer beat. The audience is waiting. They know something is coming. The waiting is part of the experience.

"Reviewing..."

Then the article appears.

This is the Kronos moment. The raw voice memo just became a real newspaper article. The headline is clean. The quotes are attributed. The photos are placed. Samu's name is on it. It reads like something a journalist wrote — but it was made from a voice memo by a 22-year-old student walking around a hackathon.

The speaker doesn't say "isn't this cool?" They let the article speak for itself. Maybe they scroll down slowly. Maybe they point at a quote: "This quote came directly from the transcript. The AI didn't invent it — it extracted it."

Then: "Five seconds from voice memo to published article."

**The feeling the audience should have:** involuntary lean-forward. The same feeling as watching a magic trick where you know it's real because you can verify every detail. They're sitting in the venue. They can see the teams mentioned. The photos show the room they're in. It's not a pre-made demo — it's their reality, transformed.

**Tone of the speaker:** Restrained wonder. Not "look what we built!" but "look what happened." The speaker is almost as amazed as the audience, which makes it feel genuine.

**Visual mood:** The transition from slides (dark, dramatic) to the live app should feel like stepping from a cinema into daylight. The app is bright, clean, typographic. The article looks like a newspaper page. This contrast — dark cinematic slides to bright living product — is itself a transformation moment.

**Pacing:** The sped-up video is fast. The Generate click is deliberate. The SSE events build tension. The article reveal is the release. Fast → deliberate → tension → release. This is the rhythm of a good magic trick.

---

### Beat 5: THE DEEPER THING [2:20 - 2:50]

The audience just felt surprise. Now we take them deeper.

"But here's the part that matters."

The quality review panel appears next to the article. Score: 64/100. The missing perspectives are highlighted. The audience sees: "2 of 5 stakeholder groups represented. Missing: judge perspective, organizer perspective."

This is the moment when the pitch goes from "impressive tech demo" to "something that changes how journalism works."

"The AI didn't just check his grammar. It caught three things."

Point at the screen. Specific. Concrete. Not abstract — "this flag, right here, says the article is missing the perspective of the people judging this event."

"Samu grabbed a judge. Recorded 30 seconds. Re-generated."

Show the improved version. Score: 81. The flag is gone.

"It took 30 seconds to add the missing voice."

This is the emotional peak. Not the technical peak — the EMOTIONAL peak. Because the audience just realized: this AI doesn't just write articles. It makes articles FAIR. It asks "who else should be heard?" — a question that most human editors never ask systematically.

The judges in the room just watched the AI flag THEIR OWN absence from an article about THEIR OWN event. And then watched it get fixed. That's personal. That's memorable. That stays.

**Tone:** Slower here. Almost reverent. This is the most important idea in the whole pitch, and it deserves space. The speaker is not selling — they're sharing something they believe matters.

**Visual mood:** The quality panel should feel medical — precise, diagnostic, actionable. Green/yellow/red scoring. The before/after scores should be side by side or animated. The gap closing (64→81) should feel like healing.

---

### Beat 6: THE GROUND [2:50 - 3:20]

Back to earth. After the emotional peak, the audience needs to feel grounded. "This is real. This can work. This is a business."

The numbers do the work. Two columns. Left: "$300,000/year — traditional newspaper." Right: "$5/year — our platform."

The contrast is so extreme it's almost funny. Let it land.

"Contributors are community members, not employees. AI costs pennies per article. Even $10 in local ads makes a town profitable."

Then the market: "20,000+ news deserts globally. Local content converts at 24% paid — eight times the national average."

Then positioning: a simple chart showing the whitespace. "Patch aggregates. Satchel serves professionals. City Bureau trains citizens. Nobody combines citizen input, AI generation, AND quality review."

This slide feels like the foundation of a building. The audience just saw the beautiful architecture (demo + quality engine). Now they see: the foundation is solid. The math works. The market exists. The gap is real.

**Tone:** Grounded. Matter-of-fact. The numbers speak with the confidence of someone who has done the work. No hype. No projections about being a billion-dollar company. Just: this works, at this cost, for this many people.

**Visual mood:** Lighter than the emotional slides. Clean white or light backgrounds. Big, readable numbers. Simple chart. The design says "we're serious" without trying hard.

---

### Beat 7: THE RESTING PLACE [3:20 - 4:00]

The close is not a crescendo. It's a resting place.

The energy comes DOWN, not up. The speaker slows. The voice is quieter.

"The old newspaper had one editor with one worldview. Our AI editor checks every story — not just whether the facts are right, but whether the community is represented."

Pause. Let it breathe.

"Local news didn't die because people stopped caring. It died because the economics broke. AI fixes the economics. But we went further — AI fixes the blind spots the old newspapers had too."

Pause again.

"Your town gets a newspaper. A better newspaper."

Then: the live URL appears on screen. Large. Readable.

"Check it. It's live right now."

The speaker stops talking. The URL is on screen. The judges take out their phones. They type it in. The article loads. Samu's article about the hackathon they're sitting in. They scroll through it. They see the quality score. They see the photos of the room they're in.

This is the resting place. The pitch is over, but the experience continues on the judges' phones. The product lives beyond the presentation. That's the point.

**Tone:** Quiet conviction. Not a rallying cry — a statement of fact. This should exist. We built it. It's live.

**Visual mood:** The projected screen shows the newspaper site. Clean. Calm. The typography says "this is permanent." After the dark, dramatic opening and the energetic demo, the close feels like arriving somewhere. The newspaper is the destination.

**The last feeling:** Not excitement. Not inspiration. Something quieter and more durable: **"This should exist. And it does."**

## THE INVISIBLE STRUCTURE

The audience will never see this structure, but the pitch follows it:

```
DARK    → LIGHT    → BRIGHT   → DEEP     → SOLID    → CALM
(loss)  → (hope)   → (proof)  → (awe)    → (trust)  → (rest)
Slide 1 → Slide 2  → Slide 4  → Slide 5  → Slide 6  → Slide 7
```

The color literally goes: black background → split screen → bright app → diagnostic panel → clean numbers → newspaper site. The visual journey mirrors the emotional journey. Loss is dark. Proof is bright. Rest is calm.

## HOW THE SPEAKER SHOULD FEEL

Not like a salesperson. Not like a hype man. Not like a CEO.

Like someone who found something broken, figured out how to fix it, built the fix, and is now showing it to people for the first time.

The best pitch energy is: **the enthusiasm of someone who can't quite believe it works.** Not performed excitement — genuine surprise at their own creation. "Look at this. Look what happens when you hit Generate. I've seen it 50 times and it still gets me."

This is authentic to hackathons. The team literally built this in 30 hours. The wonder should be real.

---

## TRANSITIONS

The transitions between slides matter as much as the slides themselves.

**Slide 1 → Slide 2:** "But..." — the simplest transition word. The weight of the problem... BUT the raw material is already there. The "but" lifts the audience out of gravity.

**Slide 2 → Slide 3:** "Until now." → "We built [Name]." — Clean cut. No transition needed. The product appears.

**Slide 3 → Slide 4:** "Let me show you." — Four words. Then: switch from slides to live app. The transition from keynote to browser is itself a statement: we're not showing slides anymore, we're showing reality.

**Slide 4 → Slide 5:** "But here's the part that matters." — The "but" again. The demo was impressive, BUT the deeper thing is the quality engine. This is the second turn. The audience thought the article was the point. The quality review is the real point.

**Slide 5 → Slide 6:** "How does this work as a business?" — The speaker asks the question the judges are thinking. Then answers it before they have to ask.

**Slide 6 → Slide 7:** No transition word. Just a shift in energy. The speaker slows down. Takes a breath. The audience feels the gear change. The numbers slide fades. The newspaper appears.

---

## WHAT THE DECK IS NOT

**Not a slideshow.** It's a performance with a screen behind it. The slides are scenery. The speaker is the experience.

**Not comprehensive.** It deliberately leaves things out. No team intro slide. No architecture diagram. No feature list. No roadmap. Leaving things out creates space for the things that matter. Every hackathon pitch that tries to cover everything covers nothing.

**Not a pitch for investors.** It's a pitch for judges who have seen 15 presentations and are tired. It must work on ENERGY and MEMORY, not on information density. The Q&A is where depth lives. The pitch is where feeling lives.

**Not neutral.** This pitch has a point of view. "The old newspaper had blind spots. Ours doesn't." That's a claim. We back it up with the demo. But the confidence to make the claim — that's what separates a winning pitch from a safe one.

---

## THE SOUND OF IT

If you recorded just the audio of this pitch — no visuals, no demo — it should sound like a short documentary narration. Measured, confident, occasionally quiet, with moments of genuine wonder.

The pace is NOT uniform:
- Slide 1: Slow. Every word weighted.
- Slide 2: Slightly faster. Building energy.
- Slide 3: Quick. Almost throwaway — "here's how it works, but let me SHOW you."
- Slide 4: Variable. Video is fast. "Watch." is a dead stop. SSE events build. Article reveal is a breath.
- Slide 5: Slow again. This matters. Give it room.
- Slide 6: Confident pace. These numbers speak for themselves.
- Slide 7: Slowest. Almost conversational. The speaker is talking TO the judges now, not AT them.

---

## THE PHYSICAL PRESENCE

The speaker stands to the side of the screen, not in front of it. During the demo, they step TOWARD the screen — they're excited about what's on it. During the close, they step AWAY from the screen and face the audience directly.

"Check it. It's live right now." — Said looking at the judges, not at the screen. This is a direct invitation. Eye contact. The screen has the URL. The speaker has the conviction.

Hands: natural. Not in pockets, not wildly gesturing. When pointing at the quality review panel, use a single finger, not a laser pointer. Touch the screen if possible — it signals ownership and intimacy with the product.

---

## ONE LAST THING

The pitch should feel like a story that was DISCOVERED, not DESIGNED.

The best pitches have a quality of inevitability — "of course this is the answer, why didn't anyone do this before?" The speaker should radiate that feeling. Not "we had a clever idea" but "we found something that was waiting to be built."

The news desert crisis is real. The raw material is real. The AI capability is new. The quality engine is the innovation. The economics work. The demo proves it. It's live right now.

Of course this should exist.

We just built it first.
