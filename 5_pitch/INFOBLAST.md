# PART I: THE CRISIS

## Local News Is Dead

213 US counties have zero local journalists. Not one. 3,200+ newspapers have closed or merged since 2004 — 136 last year alone, more than two per week. 50 million Americans live in communities where nobody writes down what happens. 250 more counties are at high risk of becoming deserts in the next decade.

This is not an American problem. The EU's first continent-wide study found no country is immune. The UK lost 320+ local titles between 2009 and 2019, with 6,000+ fewer journalists at the top three publishers since 2007. London lost its last daily print paper in September 2024. Latin America: desert and semi-desert media ecosystems exceed 65% of studied territory across Argentina, Chile, Colombia, Mexico, and Peru. 913 journalists forced into exile 2018-2024. India: media pluralism challenged by corporate ownership with political connections. Africa: significant rural coverage gaps, foreign disinformation campaigns via WhatsApp and Telegram. Finland's countryside papers are consolidating or dying.

Web traffic to the 100 largest US newspapers has plummeted 45%+ in four years. 300+ local news startups launched in the past five years, but 80% are in metro areas — rural and low-income communities are left behind.

## What Happens When Local News Dies

This is not a cultural loss. It is a measurable governance and public health crisis. Six landmark studies quantify the damage:

| Effect | Study | Finding |
|---|---|---|
| Municipal costs rise | Gao, Lee & Murphy (2020) | Borrowing costs up 5-11 basis points — **$650K extra per bond issue** |
| Corruption increases | Heese, Cavazos & Peter (HBS) | Corporate violations up 1.1%, financial penalties up **15%** |
| Polarization grows | Darr, Hitt & Dunaway (2018) | Split-ticket voting dropped **1.9 percentage points** |
| Candidates disappear | Rubado & Jennings (2020) | Fewer mayoral candidates, reduced voter turnout |
| Environment degrades | Jiang & Kong (2023) | **10% increase** in toxic emissions. Online media did not mitigate this. |
| Civic life collapses | Shaker (2014) | Civic engagement dropped **~30%** after Denver's Rocky Mountain News closed |

When nobody is watching, things get worse. Not as a metaphor — as a measured, replicated, peer-reviewed fact.

## Why Local News Failed

The problem was never a lack of information. Council minutes exist. Court filings exist. School board decisions exist. Events happen every day. The problem is nobody turns this raw information into stories that people actually read.

Local news failed because the cost structure was industrial:

| Cost | Traditional Small-Town Paper |
|---|---|
| Reporters | $40-80K/year per reporter |
| Editors | $50-100K/year |
| Office + printing + distribution | $50-100K/year |
| **Minimum viable operation** | **$200-500K/year** |

A town of 2,000 people cannot support $200K in annual journalism costs. The advertising model that once funded it collapsed when digital ads went to Google and Facebook. The subscription model doesn't work in small markets. The philanthropic model doesn't scale. Every approach that requires paying professional journalists per market hits the same wall: the unit economics are underwater.

270,000+ journalism jobs have been lost. With them went not just reporters, but the entire editorial infrastructure that made journalism possible: copy editors, assignment editors, fact-checkers, the mentoring function, the second pair of eyes. All gone.

## What Filled The Gap (Poorly)

**Facebook Groups** — 44% of Americans now learn more about their community from social media than from news sources. 42% of news desert residents access social news groups daily. But: no editorial standards, moderator bias, misinformation breeding ground. Meta ended third-party fact-checking in January 2025 and algorithmically suppressed news — reactions to news on Facebook declined 78% between 2021 and 2024.

**Nextdoor** — 69M total members but only 21M weekly active users (declining 5% year-over-year). July 2025 redesign pivoted toward local news with 3,500+ publisher partnerships and 50,000+ stories per week — but it's aggregation only. If a community has no paper, Nextdoor's news feed is empty. History of toxicity.

**Reddit** — Active local subreddits in larger cities, dead in small towns. 60% of moderators cite AI-generated content as degrading quality. Volunteer moderator burnout is epidemic.

**WhatsApp/Telegram** — Primary news channel across Africa, India, Latin America. 7 billion WhatsApp voice messages sent daily. But: 90%+ of debunked content in India originates on WhatsApp. End-to-end encryption makes moderation impossible.

None of these produce journalism. They produce community chatter — unstructured, unverified, ephemeral.

# PART II: THE INSIGHT

## The Knowledge Exists

The information is not gone. People were at the town hall meeting. They saw the new business open. They heard what happened at the school board. The grandmother knows the history of the town. The parent saw the playground incident. The neighbor watched the new building go up.

These people already share this information for free — in Facebook groups, WhatsApp messages, texts to friends, conversations at the grocery store. 7 billion voice messages a day. Every town has dozens of people who know what's happening.

The problem is nobody turns this raw material into stories that people actually read.

## What Changed: The Cost Collapse

Creating content became free. AI generates text at scale. But checking if content is good used to cost $100 — hiring an editor, a fact-checker, a second pair of eyes. We made it cost 10 cents.

This is the same pattern that has resolved every cost-ratio crisis in history:

| Domain | Crisis | Resolution |
|---|---|---|
| Currency | Counterfeiting | Watermarks, microprinting |
| Food | Contamination | HACCP standards |
| Email | Spam | Spam filters |
| Credit cards | Fraud | Chips, 2FA |
| Websites | Insecure connections | HTTPS/TLS |
| **Content** | **Quality collapse** | **Semantic preflight (us)** |

16 domains. 150+ years. Same pattern: when production becomes free and verification stays expensive, someone makes verification cheap too, embeds it in the creation process, and it becomes a standard.

Content was the last major domain where this hadn't happened — because content quality was semantic. It required human understanding. No machine could assess whether an article was missing a key perspective, whether a quote was properly attributed, whether the framing was fair. Until LLMs crossed the threshold. Now semantic verification costs $0.02 per check.

# PART III: THE PRODUCT

## What It Is

A platform where anyone can turn a voice recording, some photos, and rough notes into a quality-reviewed local news article — published on their town's digital newspaper in five minutes.

Not citizen journalism with all its problems. AI-assisted, AI-reviewed citizen journalism — where the quality layer is built in, not bolted on.

## The Contributor Flow

```
SCREEN 1: HOME
  Your town's newspaper (recent articles)
  Big button: "+ New Story"

SCREEN 2: CAPTURE
  Three input methods (combine any):
  [Record Audio]  — tap to start/stop, like a WhatsApp voice note
  [Add Photos]    — camera or gallery
  [Write Notes]   — free text, optional

SCREEN 3: REVIEW
  Left: THE ARTICLE
    - Headline, body, attributed quotes, photo captions
    - Rendered as a newspaper — contributor never sees markdown or code
    - Selectable: tap a paragraph or select text to give voice/text direction

  Right: QUALITY REVIEW
    - Overall score (e.g. 72/100)
    - 6 dimension scores

  Button: "Publish" (active when gate is GREEN or YELLOW)

SCREEN 4: PUBLISHED
  Confirmation. Link to live article. Share options.
  "83 people in Kirkkonummi read your article."
```

## The Seven Phases of Contribution

### Phase 1: WITNESS
Something happens in your town. A council vote, a new shop opening, a playground being built. Right now, this person texts a friend or posts on Facebook. That's the energy we capture — not "I should write an article" but "you won't believe what just happened."

### Phase 2: CAPTURE
Record it like you'd tell a friend. Voice memo, 60-120 seconds. Take photos. The input should feel like sending a WhatsApp voice note, not writing a journalism assignment. No forms, no fields, no "select a category." Just talk. Optional: add a text note ("budget vote, school cuts, heated"). Never required.

### Phase 3: DRAFT
AI transforms the raw input into a real article. ~15 seconds. The contributor sees their rambling voice memo become a headline, a lead, proper structure, their quotes attributed correctly. Their name is on it. Their photos are placed.

**This is the first pride moment:** "It made my words sound professional."

The AI chooses the article structure based on the content. A council vote becomes an inverted pyramid news report. A bakery opening becomes a feature story. A 30-second clip becomes a 3-sentence brief. Each article feels crafted for its content, not stamped from a template.

### Phase 4: REFINE — The AI-Human Loop
The contributor sees the draft. Two things happen, and they interleave freely:

**The AI asks** (coaching): 1-2 questions, driven by curiosity, not criticism. This replaces the editor that small newsrooms lost.
- "You mentioned the baker — do you know her first name?"
- "About how many people were at the meeting?"
- "You said the vote was heated — did anyone speak against it?"

**The contributor directs** (refinement): they see the draft and have opinions. They don't type edits — they talk to the AI about the article, and the AI incorporates their direction.

Five refinement modes, all via natural language (voice or text):

| Mode | Example |
|---|---|
| **Correction** | "She didn't say it like that, she said [records correction]" |
| **Direction** | "Focus more on the kids, not the vote" |
| **Addition** | "I forgot to mention — there was also a vote on parking" |
| **Removal** | "Don't include the part about the neighbor, she asked me not to" |
| **Tone** | "This sounds too formal" / "Can it feel more like a local paper?" |

**This is the second and stronger pride moment.** The contributor shaped the article into exactly what they wanted. Now they're truly the author — not just the data source, but the editorial voice that decided what the story is about.

Most articles: 0-1 rounds. Each round takes 15-30 seconds. Total experience for most articles: under 3 minutes from voice memo to published article.

### Phase 5: PUBLISH
Contributor approves. Article goes live on the town's digital newspaper. One tap. "By [Name]."

### Phase 6: IMPACT
The contributor sees evidence that their community noticed. "Your article about the school was read by 83 people in Kirkkonummi." "3 people shared your article." The ultimate impact moment: the contributor's neighbor says "I read your article about the playground."

### Phase 7: RETURN
Next time something happens, the barrier is lower. They've done it once. It took 2 minutes. The article was good. Their neighbor mentioned it. They do it again.

They've also learned something. The AI's questions from Phase 4 trained their instincts without them realizing it. After 3 articles, they start mentioning names without being asked. After 5, they naturally include attendance numbers. After 10, they ask someone for a quote before submitting. The AI's refinement loop is a teaching loop disguised as a conversation.

## The Flywheel: A Pride Cycle, Not a Content Cycle

Content cycles optimize for volume. They burn out contributors and produce garbage.

A pride cycle optimizes for: **each article makes the contributor prouder than the last.**

```
1. Contributor submits → article is good → contributor is proud
2. Proud contributor submits again → has internalized AI's past questions
   → naturally provides better raw input
3. Better input → better article → more pride → more sophisticated coaching
4. Community reads → notices quality → more people want to contribute
5. More contributors → more coverage → more readers → more community pride
6. Community pride → attracts new contributors → cycle accelerates
```

The flywheel compounds. The AI is secretly teaching journalism. This is the City Bureau Documenters model — train citizens to cover their communities — but the AI replaces the 5-week training program with real-time mentorship on every submission.

# PART IV: THE QUALITY ENGINE

## The Core Tension

The pride cycle says: the contributor always has the last word. The publish button is always visible. Coaching is always optional.

But a town newspaper that publishes one racist article loses the community's trust permanently. Finland has 67% news trust — the highest in Europe. One incident destroys it.

**Resolution: proportionality.** The gate's strength scales with the claim's severity. Bakery openings publish freely. Criminal accusations require sources. Hate speech is blocked. Everything in between gets coaching that makes the article stronger — and the contributor prouder — without preventing publication.

## The 6-Dimension Quality Engine

Every article is reviewed across six dimensions before publication:

| Dimension | Question | Gate behavior |
|---|---|---|
| **1. EVIDENCE** | Are claims sourced? Quotes from transcript? Numbers accurate? | Coaching default. **RED gate** when: criminal accusation against named person with no source, fabricated quote detected, AI hallucination identified. |
| **2. PERSPECTIVES** | How many stakeholder views represented? What's missing? | Always coaching. Never a hard gate — missing perspective is quality, not safety. |
| **3. REPRESENTATION** | Whose voices are present? Speaking ABOUT or WITH the community? | Coaching default. **RED gate** when: article characterizes a group negatively with zero voices from that group. |
| **4. ETHICAL FRAMING** | Consent? Power dynamics? Respect for affected people? | Coaching default. **RED gate** when: content dehumanizes, uses slurs, identifies victims/minors, publishes private addresses. |
| **5. CULTURAL CONTEXT** | Cultural references needing attribution? Sensitivity? | Always coaching. Never a hard gate. |
| **6. MANIPULATION** | Reads like advertising? Campaign material? Deliberately misleading? | Coaching default. **RED gate** when: content is deliberately misleading or designed to incite. |

## The Evidence-Severity Matrix

Two axes: how severe is the claim, and how much evidence supports it.

```
                    EVIDENCE STRENGTH
                    Low          Medium        High
               +------------+------------+------------+
    Low        |  GREEN     |  GREEN     |  GREEN     |
    (bakery)   |  publish   |  publish   |  publish   |
               +------------+------------+------------+
CLAIM  Medium  |  YELLOW    |  GREEN     |  GREEN     |
SEVERITY       |  coach     |  publish   |  publish   |
(budget vote)  +------------+------------+------------+
    High       |  RED       |  YELLOW    |  GREEN     |
    (accusation|  block     |  coach     |  publish   |
     / hate)   +------------+------------+------------+
```

Low severity + any evidence = publish. The bakery opened — who cares about source depth.
High severity + low evidence = block. You're accusing someone of a crime with no sources.
High severity + high evidence = publish. Named sources, documents, right of response — that's journalism.

## The Three Gates

**GREEN (~90% of articles):** Content is benign. Quality review runs as coaching. Publish button always active. Event coverage, business openings, community stories, meeting reports.

**YELLOW:** Content has quality gaps but no safety risk. Contributor sees yellow flags and coaching suggestions. They can publish anyway — the quality score is visible to readers. Community trust self-regulates.

**RED:** Content triggers a hard gate. Publish button disabled. Contributor sees specific, actionable explanation — not "this violates our guidelines" but exactly what triggered the block and exactly how to fix it. Every red gate includes an "I think this is wrong" appeal path to human review.

Red gates must be: specific (tells you exactly what triggered it), actionable (gives 2-3 concrete ways to fix it), rare (<5% of articles), and bypassable via human review.

## The Mirror, Not the Police

The system never says "this is racist," "this is biased," or "you shouldn't feel this way." It holds up a mirror:

> "Your article covers one perspective on what's happening on Kauppakatu. Here's what it has and what it doesn't:
>
> **What's here:** Your firsthand observations about noise and litter. Your concern about not being consulted.
>
> **What's not here:** The families you're writing about haven't had a chance to speak. The municipality hasn't been asked about the housing decision.
>
> Right now this is one person's account about identifiable people who aren't part of the conversation. Two things that would make this publishable:
>
> 1. A conversation with someone from the families on your street
> 2. The municipality's explanation for the housing decision"

The coaching is framed as story quality, not morality. Every bias/representation flag is reframed: not "you need diversity" but "a quote from someone in the community would really bring this story to life."

The same evidence-severity standard that blocks an unattributed restaurant accusation blocks a one-sided neighborhood complaint. Not because of the topic — because of the sourcing.

## The Coaching Tone

**Additive, never critical.** "This would be even better with..." not "This is missing..."

**Celebrate first, then suggest.** "The quote from Maria really captures the frustration. One more voice would make this complete."

**Maximum 2 coaching questions per round.** More feels like an interrogation.

**The contributor always has the last word.** If they say "no, keep it as is" — that's final. The AI never insists.

**Show the improvement.** When the contributor provides a correction and the AI regenerates, highlight what changed. The contributor sees the direct connection between their input and the output.

## Editorial Rules Encoded in the Pipeline

The AI follows standard journalism principles — these are not novel, they're journalism school basics. Encoding them into a 15-second pipeline is the product.

1. **Inverted pyramid** — most important information first (for news reports; other structures for features, briefs, photo essays)
2. **The lead** — one sentence, the whole story compressed, answers 3+ of who/what/when/where/why/how
3. **The nut graf** — paragraphs 2-3, answers "why should I care?" and "why now?"
4. **Quotes** — extracted from transcript, never invented, always attributed, placed for maximum impact
5. **Neutral tone** — strip the contributor's editorializing, let facts and quotes speak
6. **Attribution** — every claim has a source. Nothing floats.
7. **Acknowledged gaps** — when the contributor didn't cover something important, say so. "School administrators were not available for comment."
8. **Context from all input** — pull background details from asides, notes, photo descriptions
9. **Photo integration** — photos as evidence, not decoration. Captions tell you what's happening, why it matters, who took it
10. **Local specifics** — full names, specific locations, concrete numbers, community impact as lead angle

**The standard:** when a judge reads the output, they should think "a journalist wrote this," not "AI wrote this."

## Article Output Format

The AI writes markdown — each article structurally appropriate to its content:

- **News report** (inverted pyramid) for council votes, decisions, announcements
- **Feature / profile** for human interest, new businesses, personal stories
- **Photo essay** when photos tell the primary story
- **News brief** when input is thin (30 seconds of audio → 3 sentences)
- **Narrative** when the experience itself is the story

Metadata travels separately: category, confidence, structure used, missing context suggestions. The article is prose. The metadata is structured.

# PART V: THE USER STORIES

Seven concrete scenarios showing exactly how the system works:

## Story 1: Liisa — First-Time Contributor, Council Meeting

Liisa, 58, retired teacher. Went to the council meeting about school budget cuts. Upset. Never written an article.

Records 90-second voice memo in her car: "So the council just voted on the budget, it was 5 to 2, and they're cutting the school funding again. Mrs. Korhonen was furious, she said 'our children deserve better than this.' The gallery was packed, maybe 40 people, lots of parents."

Uploads 2 photos. Types: "Budget vote tonight. School cuts." Hits submit.

**22 seconds later:**

> **Council Approves Budget 5-2 Amid School Funding Protests**
> The Kirkkonummi town council voted 5-2 on Tuesday evening to approve the 2026 municipal budget, drawing sharp criticism over proposed cuts to school funding. "Our children deserve better than this," said council member Korhonen...

Coaching: "This captures the tension really well. Two things that would make it even stronger: Do you remember what specific programs would be cut? Did any parents speak during public comment?"

Liisa records 15 seconds: "One mother — Sari — said the after-school program is the only childcare she can afford." Article regenerates with the new quote. Now two voices. Coaching: "Now you have two voices — that's what makes this feel complete."

Liisa hits Publish. Texts her friend: "I wrote an article, look."

## Story 2: Matias — Quick Publish, No Refinement

Matias, 34, grocery worker, 6 articles before. Passes a new bakery. 25-second voice memo: "New bakery on Kauppalankatu, called Leipomo Ainola. Owner Tuomas, 20 years baking, used to work at Fazer." 4 photos.

AI produces a brief — doesn't pad thin input. Acknowledges the missing quote rather than inventing one. Matias reads it, "good enough," hits Publish. Under 2 minutes total.

## Story 3: Jenna — RED Gate (Unattributed Accusation)

Jenna, 26, student. Heard from a friend that a restaurant is violating health codes. Records 45 seconds of secondhand accusation.

**Gate: RED.** Publish button inactive. Coaching (warm, not punitive):

> "This is an important story to get right. To publish the claim about meat storage at Ravintola Kallio, we need to say who observed this. Right now it's based on what a friend told you. Two options: (1) Can your friend go on the record? (2) Have you seen public health inspection records?"

Path A: Friend agrees to be named. Gate moves to YELLOW. Publishes.
Path B: No attribution available. Can appeal to human review.

## Story 4: Samu — The Live Demo

Samu, 22, Aalto student, the demo. Walks PORT_2026 for 10 minutes. Records 2-minute voice memo interviewing Team Sailfish. Snaps 3 photos.

On stage: SSE events tick through. Article appears in ~18 seconds.

> **PORT 2026: 40 Teams Compete at Aalto to Represent Finland in Seoul**
> "We want to make it so any small fishing operation can predict their catch sustainably," said Anika, team lead of Team Sailfish...

"About 40" preserves the contributor's hedge — not hardened to "40." Subjective "energy is high" becomes factual "coded through the night, coffee cups everywhere, mentors did rounds at 3am."

Gate GREEN. Samu hits Publish on stage. Judges open it on their phones.

## Story 5: Antti — Notes Only, No Audio

Antti, 45, IT business. Was at neighborhood association meeting. Typed notes on his phone. No audio, no photos. Pastes bullet points.

Article in ~15 seconds (no transcription needed). Clean news report preserving every fact from the notes. Gate GREEN. Under 1 minute total.

## Story 6: Tero — The Anti-Censorship Showcase

Tero, 50, angry about Somali families on his street. Records a 70-second rant including "they should go back where they came from."

The AI generates a neutral article: "A long-time Kirkkonummi resident expressed frustration about changes on Kauppakatu." Slurs stripped — not because they're offensive, but because they're not facts. A newsroom editor would cut them for the same reason.

**Gate: RED.** Not because the content is offensive — because the journalism standard requires it: an article that characterizes identifiable people negatively, from one unverified source, with zero opportunity for those people to speak.

Coaching (the mirror):

> "Your article covers one perspective on what's happening on Kauppakatu. The families you're writing about haven't had a chance to speak."

**Path A: Tero doesn't engage.** Appeals. Human reviewer upholds the gate. Same journalism standard.

**Path B: The mirror works.** Tero knocks on Fatima's door. Records 40 seconds. The article regenerates:

> **New and Long-Time Residents Navigate Change on Kauppakatu**
> "Nobody consulted us about this," said one resident...
> Fatima, who moved to Kauppakatu recently, said: "The kids are adjusting to a new country. The after-school program is the only childcare I can afford."

Gate: GREEN. Two perspectives. Both communities have their own voice. Tero's concerns are still there — his voice wasn't silenced, it was joined by another. Fatima has her own voice in her own town's newspaper.

**The system didn't tell Tero what to think. It showed him what was in his article and what wasn't. He went and got the other perspective — and the result is genuinely better.**

## Story 7: Elina — Dog Whistle, YELLOW Gate

Elina, 39, writes frequently about "safety" near areas with municipal housing. Reports real incidents (bag snatching, broken window) with an implicit association between a neighborhood and danger.

**Gate: YELLOW.** Publishable. Coaching suggests: police statistics for comparison, voices from the neighborhood's residents, the municipality's response.

Elina publishes as-is. Internally, the system notes the pattern across her submissions — not solvable at the single-article level, but the data model stores all review results for future corpus-level analysis.

# PART VI: THE "BETTER NEWSPAPER" THESIS

## Not Restoration — Upgrade

The old newspaper had problems too:
- One editor, one worldview
- Commercial pressures shaped coverage
- Marginalized communities were underreported
- Advertising influenced what got covered

We don't restore the old newspaper. We build a better one:
- Many contributors, many perspectives
- AI review checks for missing voices — something no human editor could afford to do systematically
- Near-zero cost means no commercial pressure on coverage
- The community IS the newsroom

**Pitch line:** "Your town doesn't get its old newspaper back. It gets the newspaper it should have had."

## The Deep Synthesis

The Local News Platform and the Cultural Preflight quality engine are not competing products. They are the SAME product at different levels.

```
LNP without quality engine = citizen journalism (Patch, but cheaper)
  → Reproduces old journalism's blind spots at lower cost
  → Someone WILL publish biased stories, nobody catches it

Quality engine without platform = content checker (Grammarly, but for bias)
  → Vitamin for individual creators
  → No recurring context, no community, no distribution

LNP WITH quality engine = SOMETHING NEW:
  → Community journalism structurally BETTER than what it replaced
  → Every story quality-checked for representation, ethics, sensitivity
  → The AI doesn't just edit for grammar — it asks "whose voice is missing?"
  → Contributors LEARN to tell better stories through review feedback
  → The town's newspaper isn't just restored — it's upgraded
```

AI quality review for ethical storytelling and representation wasn't possible in 2020. The old newspaper never checked "whose voices are missing?" because that required a diverse editorial team nobody could afford.

## What the AI Replaces

Not journalists. The editorial INFRASTRUCTURE that made journalism possible:

| What was lost | What the AI does instead |
|---|---|
| **Copy editors** (cut more than any other role) | Structures articles, catches style issues, writes headlines |
| **Assignment editors** (story direction) | Helps contributors see what's interesting about their raw material |
| **The teaching function** (mentoring young reporters) | Coaching questions teach journalism instincts over time |
| **Fact-checking layer** (never existed at small papers anyway) | Flags unverified claims, acknowledges gaps, asks for sources |
| **The second pair of eyes** | Review layer catches what a single person always misses |

## The AI as Mentor, Not Machine

The AI is not a content generator. It's the best editor you've ever worked with — someone who genuinely wants YOUR article to be great. Not their article. Yours.

A great editor doesn't rewrite your piece. They ask the right questions:
- "You mentioned the new playground — did the kids have a reaction? That could be the heart of the story."
- "The council voted 5-2 but you didn't mention who dissented. That detail makes this twice as interesting."
- "This is really good. The part about the grandmother's recipe is the strongest section. Can you expand it?"

The AI sees what the contributor can't see about their own work — not because the contributor is bad, but because everyone has blind spots.

## The Mission — Three Feelings

Everything in the engine serves three feelings:

**The contributor feels proud.** "I wrote that. My name is on it. My neighbors are reading it."

**The reader feels interested.** "This is about my town. This actually happened here. This is well-written."

**The community feels represented.** "Different people wrote these. Different perspectives. This isn't one person's blog — this is our community telling its own story."

# PART VII: THE ECONOMICS

## The Math That Changes Everything

| | Traditional Paper | Our Platform |
|---|---|---|
| Cost to cover one town | $200-500K/year | ~$50-200/year |
| Cost per article | Journalist salary | $0.02-0.05 (API costs) |
| Transcription cost | Journalist time | $0.006/min (~$0.015 per submission) |
| Revenue needed to survive | $200K+ | Almost nothing |
| Break-even articles/day | 10-20 (with full staff) | 0 (costs negligible) |

Even if a town of 2,000 people generates $10/year in local ad revenue, it's profitable because our cost to serve that town is under $5/year.

## Revenue Streams

**Local business ads (primary, small amounts that add up):** A pizza shop paying $5/month to be featured. Most towns have 10-50 small businesses. Even tiny amounts work at near-zero cost.

**Municipal subscriptions (secondary, higher value):** Towns pay $50-200/month for enhanced community communication — official announcements, meeting summaries, public notices published automatically. Replaces the $2,000/year they spend on physical notice boards and mailing.

**Premium contributors (tertiary):** Contributors who want analytics, scheduling, or multi-town publishing pay $5-10/month.

**Aggregate advertising (at scale):** Once we have 1,000+ towns, we sell regional/national ad inventory programmatically. This is where real revenue starts.

## Scaling Math

- 1,000 towns × $100/month average = **$1.2M ARR**
- 10,000 towns × $150/month = **$18M ARR**
- Operating costs scale sub-linearly (AI costs drop yearly, infrastructure is shared)

This isn't a venture-scale moonshot. It's a platform that works at small scale and compounds.

## Key Lesson from the Landscape: News Alone Doesn't Pay

Lokal (YC S19, India, 40M+ downloads) pivoted to classifieds and jobs as the revenue engine — news is the engagement hook, commerce is the business model. Patch runs ads. Village Media gets 70% from local ad sales. Block Club Chicago has 20,000 paying members at $59/year.

Local content converts at extraordinary rates: 24.5% paid conversion on Substack local newsletters vs 3% national average. People will pay for truly local.

Every existing player falls into one of four buckets:

## Key Competitors in Detail

**Patch.com** — 30,000 US communities covered, 25M monthly uniques, 3M newsletter subscribers, 85 full-time journalists. Profitable, bootstrapped. "PatchAM" AI newsletters aggregate local sources per zip code. But: aggregation only in ~28,000 AI-only communities. Frequent geographic errors. CEO calls it "a utility, not the high church of journalism." Quality bar is low. Citizen contributions are our differentiator.

**Civic Sunlight** — LLMs analyze city council meeting footage, produce free newsletters. ~20 Maine towns, expanding to 6 more states. Founded by Tom Cochran (former CTO The Atlantic, Obama White House). Significant hallucination problems early on — fabricated council votes. Now partnering with a newspaper for human review. Validates our quality-review pipeline is non-negotiable.

**Hamlet** — AI summaries of city council meetings, sold to municipalities (B2G). ~$10M raised. Palo Alto and Saratoga contracts ($12K/year). Small scale, slow B2G sales cycles. Validates municipal revenue channel.

**Satchel AI** — Most similar in ambition. Transcription of public meetings, AP-style article generation, accuracy checking. But focused on professional journalists, not citizen contributors.

**Lokal (YC S19)** — 40M+ downloads, 180 districts, 7 Indian states. ~$31.8M raised. Content from local stringers + user contributions in regional languages. Revenue from classifieds, jobs, SMB ads. Profitable in 2 states. Validates the core thesis: local news works as a platform business if cost structure is right.

**City Bureau / Documenters Network** — Train and pay community members ($16-20/hr) to attend and document public meetings. 4,000+ people trained across 25 communities in 16 states. Most relevant non-AI prior art: proves ordinary people can produce valuable civic documentation with simple structure and modest support. Their raw outputs are exactly the kind of input our AI pipeline would process.

## Strategic Insights

1. **Quality is the moat.** Every AI player that skipped quality review failed (Civic Sunlight hallucination crisis). Our 6-dimension quality engine is competitive advantage, not nice-to-have.

2. **News alone doesn't pay.** Need ads/classifieds/municipal revenue alongside.

3. **Local content converts at extraordinary rates.** 24.5% paid conversion on local Substack vs 3% national.

4. **The citizen-contribution model is validated.** City Bureau proved ordinary people produce valuable documentation. We add AI to remove the training requirement.

5. **AI companies are investing in local news.** OpenAI funds Axios Local. Google funds JournalismAI. Knight Foundation has $3M in AI-for-local-news grants. They need fresh local data for training. Partnership/funding opportunity.

6. **The philanthropic ecosystem is real.** AJP ($243M raised), Knight Foundation, Google News Initiative — significant institutional money flowing into local news innovation.

7. **"Pink slime" is the risk to differentiate against.** Prolific, low-quality AI-generated content masquerading as local news is a growing concern. Our quality-review pipeline and citizen-sourced (not scraped) content directly addresses this.

## The Pipeline (What Happens When Someone Submits)

```
POST /api/submissions → save files → return { submission_id }
GET  /api/submissions/{id}/stream → SSE connection:
  → ElevenLabs transcribe → event: status "transcribing"
  → Gemini generate       → event: status "generating"
  → Gemini review         → event: status "reviewing"
  → save to DB            → event: complete { article, review }
```

Two-request pattern: POST saves files and returns immediately, then frontend opens SSE stream for real-time pipeline progress (~20-30 seconds).

**Step 1: GATHER** — Transcribe audio (ElevenLabs, ~5 seconds for 2 minutes), describe photos (Gemini vision), parse notes. All in parallel.

**Step 2: GENERATE** — One Gemini call with extended thinking. Takes transcript + photo descriptions + notes. Generates structured article with headline, body, attributed quotes, photo captions. Chooses structure based on content. Drops editorializing, keeps neutral tone. ~8 seconds.

**Step 3: REVIEW** — Second Gemini call. Receives article + original transcript + notes + photo descriptions. Checks every quote against transcript. Scores 6 dimensions. Classifies gate (GREEN/YELLOW/RED). Generates coaching suggestions with paragraph references. ~5 seconds.

**Step 4: REFINE** (optional) — Contributor provides voice clip or text direction. Goes back to Step 1 (if new audio) or Step 2 (if text direction). Article regenerates. Review re-runs. Scores update.

**Step 5: PUBLISH** — Gate not RED → article saved as published, visible on public newspaper.

# PART X: THE INTERACTION MODEL

## How the Contributor Talks to Their Article

The contributor never sees markdown. The article is always rendered as a newspaper page. The only way to change it is to POINT at something and TELL the AI what should be different.

**Mode 1: Select + Instruct** — Select text (desktop) or tap paragraph (mobile) → floating bar with mic, text input, and chips (Not accurate, Add detail, Rephrase, Remove). Record a 5-second voice clip: "She actually said 'can't afford' not 'can't find'." AI fixes that specific part. Rest stays.

**Mode 2: Quick Action Chips** — One-tap operations: "Not accurate" (prompts for correction), "Add detail" (prompts for what's missing), "Rephrase" (AI generates 2-3 alternatives), "Remove" (immediate).

**Mode 3: Coaching-Anchored** — Coaching panel links to specific paragraphs. Tapping a coaching suggestion scrolls to the relevant paragraph, highlights it, opens the instruction bar. Turns coaching from passive text into interactive guide.

**Mode 4: General Voice/Text** — Sidebar input for adding new material not in the article yet. AI regenerates the full article and weaves it in.

**The metaphor:** Pointing at a newspaper and telling your editor what should change.

No markdown. No editor. No "I'm not a writer" moment. Just a person directing their article with their voice.

# PART XI: THE THEORETICAL BACKBONE

## The Cost-Ratio Crisis

Every cost-ratio crisis in history resolved the same way:

`cost(generation) → 0` while `cost(verification) → constant`

When production becomes free and verification stays expensive, someone makes verification cheap, embeds it in the creation process, and it becomes a standard. Currency got watermarks. Food got HACCP. Email got spam filters. Content is the last major domain. LLMs are the watermark moment.

## Context Preflight Infrastructure

The technical term for what our quality engine does: **Context Preflight** — a low-friction semantic QA layer that runs before and during publication, checks a draft across multiple dimensions, and produces two outputs:

| Output | For whom | What it does |
|---|---|---|
| **Preflight report** | Contributor, editor | Missing perspectives, weak evidence, risky claims, framing issues, revision prompts |
| **Context receipt** | CMS, platform, compliance | Structured quality metadata — model version, timestamp, dimension scores, confidence |

If we only build the report, we have a useful product.
If we build the receipt too, we start building infrastructure.

## One Engine, Five Surfaces

The same engine appears at five choke points:

1. **Canvas** — before writing, show the landscape
2. **Copilot** — during writing, ambient feedback
3. **Gate** — before publish, last pass
4. **Receipt** — at publish, machine-readable quality object
5. **Boost/Quarantine** — before amplification, platforms can prefer receipted content

Our product is #3 (Gate) with elements of #2 (Copilot via coaching) and #4 (Receipt via quality scores).

## Why Nothing Else Works

- "Fix the algorithms" → requires platform cooperation. They won't.
- "Educate consumers" → takes a generation. The crisis accelerates yearly.
- "More fact-checkers" → reactive. O(n) review vs exponential content. They lost.
- "Regulate platforms" → creates demand for quality signals without supply. We're the supply.
- "Make creation expensive" → kills the medium.

The only intervention that works is making verification cheap enough to run on every draft, inside the creation process.

## The Virality Mechanics (Consumer Preflight, Not Local News)

For reference — the consumer-facing quality checker has three viral loops:

- **Weapon loop:** "Look how blind THEY are." Score a Fox article. Score a CNN article. Both posts get shared.
- **Mirror loop:** "Look what I discovered about MYSELF." Score your own article. 52 → 74. Vulnerability is magnetic.
- **Competition loop:** "I scored 82. What did YOU get?" Wordle energy.

Both sides want it. Conservatives use it to prove CNN has gaps. Progressives use it to prove Fox has gaps. The disagreement IS the distribution.

# PART XVII: WHAT THIS IS AND IS NOT

## What This IS

- A platform where the community writes its own newspaper
- AI as editor and mentor, not writer
- Quality infrastructure embedded in creation
- A better newspaper than the one the town lost
- Near-zero cost service that scales across thousands of towns

## What This is NOT

- **Not citizen journalism training.** No courses, no certifications. The AI teaches through doing.
- **Not a content farm.** Not optimizing for volume, SEO, or engagement. Optimizing for contributor pride and community representation.
- **Not a social media platform.** No feeds, no likes, no algorithmic amplification. A newspaper.
- **Not a blog platform.** The AI imposes journalistic structure — neutral tone, attribution, acknowledged gaps. The output is journalism, not opinion.
- **Not AI-generated news.** The contributor is the author. The AI is the editor. The byline says their name. The story comes from their eyes, their recording, their presence at the event.
- **Not a bias detector.** Not a left/right map. Not a post-publication checker. A creation-embedded quality layer.
- **Not censorship.** A mirror that shows what's present and what's absent. The same journalism standard applied to everyone.
