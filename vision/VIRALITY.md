### 1. Score Anything by URL

Not just your own drafts — ANY article. People will IMMEDIATELY score the articles they're arguing about. Fox News on immigration. NYT on gun control. That viral thread everyone's fighting about.

The tool becomes the **referee in every online argument about media quality.** Every argument is a distribution event. Both sides share the results — because both sides think the other side is blind.

### 3. The Challenge

"Score above 80 on your own article. I bet you can't." Creator-to-creator challenge. Gamification. People post their scores, tag other creators. "Hey @rival, I scored 82 on my housing piece. What did you get?" Competition IS distribution.

### 4. The Badge

Creators who score well put the badge on their article: "Checked — 87/100." Like a blue checkmark but for substance, not identity. Once SOME creators badge, unbadged content looks unchecked. The badge creates social pressure to adopt. Every badged article is an ad for the tool.

### 5. "What's YOUR Score?"

The question that spreads at every dinner, every newsroom, every journalism class. "Have you tried [tool]? What did you score?" Same mechanic as "What's your Wordle score?" — a daily social comparison that costs nothing to participate in.

The tool goes viral **not because people agree on what good media is, but because they DISAGREE.** The disagreement IS the distribution.

- Conservatives share: "Look — CNN's climate article scores 3/9, missing the economic cost perspective entirely!"
- Progressives share: "Look — Fox's immigration piece scores 2/9, zero mention of asylum seekers!"
- Both sides use the SAME tool to prove the OTHER side is blind

The tool is the rare product that **both sides of a political divide actively want to use.** Not because it's neutral — because it's a WEAPON for everyone. Everyone thinks THEY'RE the complete ones and the other side has blind spots. The tool lets them prove it. And in the process, both sides discover their OWN blind spots too.

## THE ANTI-CENSORSHIP DESIGN

The viral mechanics only work if the tool CANNOT be accused of censorship. Design constraints:

- **SHOW, NEVER TELL.** "This perspective exists and isn't in your draft" — never "you should add this."
- **No minimum score.** Publish at 1/9 if you want. The tool doesn't care.
- **No "fix" button.** The tool never generates content. It shows gaps. You write (or don't).
- **"I disagree" button.** Flag any perspective as irrelevant. The tool respects editorial judgment.
- **Opinion mode.** Opinion pieces are expected to take a side. The tool adjusts.
- **Full transparency.** See exactly HOW and WHY the tool identified each perspective.

**The golden line:** "We don't tell you what to write. We show you what's out there. Like a map — it shows you the roads. You choose where to drive."
