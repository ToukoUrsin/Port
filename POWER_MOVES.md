# POWER MOVES

How to make the win at PORT_2026 feel inevitable.

---

## 1. Email Every Finnish Journalist on Saturday Morning

We have an email list of virtually every journalist and reporter in Finland. Saturday morning, send a short, honest message:

> "We're a team at PORT_2026 building an AI platform that turns citizen contributions into quality-reviewed local news articles. The platform is live right now. We'd love a 2-minute reaction — would something like this matter for communities that have lost their local paper?"

Include the link to the live platform. Let responses accumulate. By Sunday's pitch:

- "We reached out to X Finnish journalists yesterday. Y responded within 24 hours. Here's what they said."
- Display real quotes from real journalists on screen during the pitch.
- If even one journalist actually tries the platform and submits content, mention it by name: "A reporter at [publication] tested our platform and produced this article in 8 minutes."

No other team has access to this list. No other team can manufacture industry validation in 48 hours.

---

## 2. Build It in Finnish

Every other team will demo in English. We build in Finnish from the start — Finnish UI, Finnish article generation, Finnish quality review.

The judges are Finnish. The journalists we're emailing are Finnish. The towns we're serving are Finnish. When we show a Finnish-language article about a real Finnish community, the emotional distance drops to zero. This isn't an abstract global pitch — it's "Kirkkonummi has a newspaper again."

Whisper transcribes Finnish. Claude writes Finnish. This costs us zero extra engineering but makes everything land harder.

---

## 3. Launch a Real Town Newspaper During the Hackathon

Pick a small Finnish town that has actually lost its local paper. Spin up a real site — `[town].ourdomain.fi`. Seed it with 2-3 articles generated from publicly available data: council meeting minutes, event calendars, community announcements. All public information, restructured into real articles.

During the pitch, judges open their phones and visit the URL. A real newspaper for a real town that hasn't had one in years. Live, right now, because we built it this weekend.

---

## 4. Get Non-Team Contributors at PORT_

Don't just have Samu report the hackathon. Recruit 3-5 other people at PORT_ — mentors, organizers, participants from other teams — to submit content through the app. Walk them through it. It takes 5 minutes.

By pitch time: "We launched 20 hours ago. 5 people who are not on our team published articles about this event using our platform."

Judges can verify every article. They know the contributors. The product sold itself without our pitch.

---

## 5. Cover PORT_ Better Than PORT_

By Sunday morning, have 8-10 articles about the hackathon itself published on the platform. Workshops, teams, venue, food, keynote previews, interview snippets. A complete event newspaper.

On stage: "PORT_ doesn't have a media team. We gave them one. In 48 hours. For free. Every event, every community, every town — same thing."

The hackathon organizers become our first success story without us asking permission.

---

## 6. The Before/After That Kills

Find a real post from a Finnish local Facebook group — someone describing a town council meeting or local event in rambling, informal text. Screenshot it. Run the same information through our pipeline. Show them side by side on screen.

Left: the Facebook post. Right: the AI-generated article with structure, quotes, context, and quality review.

No explanation needed. The visual contrast does all the work.

---

## 7. Cold-Email Finnish Municipalities on Saturday

Short email to 5-10 small Finnish municipalities:

> "We're building a free digital community newspaper for towns without local news coverage. AI turns citizen contributions into reviewed articles. Would your municipality be interested in a pilot?"

Even one "tell me more" reply = real municipal interest we can show on stage. Towns already spend money on community communication — physical notice boards, mailings, dead PDF newsletters. We're offering to replace all of that.

---

## 8. Live Numbers on Stage

Show a real-time dashboard during the pitch: articles published, contributors, words generated, quality reviews completed. Not mock data — actual numbers from the weekend.

Even small numbers are devastating: "12 articles, 6 contributors, 2 towns, 0 team members needed for editorial review." Every other team shows zero real usage. We show a product with actual throughput.

---

## 9. The Recursive Demo

Record the pitch itself. Run the audio through the pipeline on stage. Publish an article about the pitch session in front of the judges. The product demonstrates itself in real time, about the moment it's being demonstrated.

If timing is tight, do this with the Q&A instead — record a judge's question, generate an article snippet from it, show it appearing live. The recursion makes the demo unforgettable.

---

## 10. Stack Everything

Each move is good alone. Together they create a gap no other team can close:

- A launched product (not a prototype)
- In Finnish (not English-only)
- With real users who aren't on the team
- Validated by working journalists in the target industry
- Serving a real town that actually needs it
- With municipal interest already expressed
- Covering the event judges are sitting in
- Showing real usage metrics
- Demonstrating itself recursively on stage

Other teams will show what they built. We show what's already working. The judges' question shifts from "which project is best?" to "this one is already real."

# POWER MOVES — WAVE 3

Waves 1 and 2 are about being the best hackathon project. Wave 3 is about not being a hackathon project at all.

The insight: a dominant win doesn't come from doing hackathon things better. It comes from operating in a category the other teams aren't in. They're building demos. We're creating real-world consequences that exist independently of PORT_2026. If the hackathon disappeared tonight, our newspapers would still be live, our journalists would still have responded, our towns would still have coverage.

That's what makes the win feel unfair. Not better execution — a different game entirely.

## 26. Generate a Newspaper for Every Journalist's Hometown

Don't just email journalists asking for feedback. Send each one a link to a newspaper FOR THEIR OWN TOWN.

Before emailing, cross-reference the journalist list with their publication locations. For each town, auto-generate 2-3 articles from publicly available municipal data (council decisions, event calendars, public notices — all online for Finnish municipalities). Spin up a page for each town.

The email becomes: "Hi [Name], we noticed [Your Town] hasn't had dedicated local news coverage in [X years]. We built one: [url]. It has 3 articles sourced from public municipal records. It took our AI 4 minutes."

This isn't a pitch for feedback. This is a gift. Someone built something for THEIR community, personally. The response rate will be dramatically higher than a generic email because it's not generic — it's about their town, their people, their beat.

On stage: "We generated personalized local newspapers for 200 Finnish towns overnight. Each one has real articles from real public data. Total API cost: €12.40."

## 27. 100 Towns by Sunday

There are ~300 municipalities in Finland. Many have limited or no dedicated local news. Council minutes, public notices, event listings — all publicly available on each municipality's website.

Batch-generate newspapers for 100 of them. Template site, subdomain routing, automated content pipeline. Each town gets 2-3 articles pulled from their own public records and transformed into readable news.

"We launched this weekend. Finland has 100 new local newspapers this morning."

The scale is the argument. No pitch deck, no TAM slide, no projection can match the visual of 100 live URLs each serving a real community. The marginal cost per town is cents. The total cost is pocket change. The economics aren't theoretical — they're proven by the fact that 100 newspapers exist right now for the cost of a lunch.

## 41. The Negative Counterfactual

Don't just show what the platform DOES. Show what happens WITHOUT it.

Find a real case: a Finnish town council voted to increase property taxes, or approved a controversial zoning decision, or cut a school program. It's in the public minutes. Nobody reported it. Residents found out weeks or months later through word of mouth.

Show the council minutes (boring PDF). Show the date it happened. Show that no article exists about it anywhere online. Then show the article our AI generates from those minutes — dated today, but about something that happened months ago.

"This decision affected 4,000 residents. Nobody told them. Not because nobody cared — because there was no newspaper. Here's the article that should have existed."

The counterfactual makes the problem visceral. Not "local news is important" in the abstract. THIS specific thing happened to THESE specific people and NOBODY TOLD THEM.

# DESTROYING WEAKNESSES

Every strong idea has attack surfaces. The move isn't to hide them — it's to destroy each one through demonstration so thoroughly that the objection evaporates before a judge can voice it.

---

## W1. "AI Will Hallucinate — Articles Will Have Fabricated Facts"

**The fear:** AI invents quotes, fabricates details, hallucinates plausible-sounding nonsense.

**Destroy it:** Show the source panel. Every article has a "view sources" button that displays the original transcript, photos, and notes side-by-side with the generated text. Every quote in the article is highlighted with a link to the exact timestamp in the audio where the person said it.

Then show a case where the AI TRIED to embellish and the quality review caught it. "The AI initially wrote 'the council unanimously approved the budget.' The review flagged this — the source transcript says 'the council approved the budget' but doesn't mention the vote count. The AI was asked to remove the word 'unanimously.'"

The system catching its own mistakes is more convincing than the system being perfect.

---

## W2. "Anyone Can Publish Anything — What About Misinformation?"

**The fear:** Bad actors use the platform to spread lies about local businesses, politicians, or neighbors.

**Destroy it:** Show the AI quality review rejecting an article in real time. Prepare a test submission with a clearly problematic claim — something defamatory or unverifiable. Run it through the pipeline on stage. Watch the AI flag it, explain why, and refuse to publish without contributor edits.

"We submitted an article claiming a local business was involved in tax fraud. The AI flagged it: 'This claim requires documentary evidence. Please provide a link to a public filing or official statement.' The article cannot publish until the contributor addresses this."

Then show the stats: "The AI reviewed 47 articles this weekend. It flagged 12 for potential issues. 4 had unverified factual claims. 2 had potentially defamatory content. 1 had a quote that didn't match the transcript. Each was sent back with specific feedback."

The quality layer isn't theoretical — it's operational, with logs.

---

## W3. "This Isn't Real Journalism"

**The fear:** AI-generated articles from amateur contributors aren't journalism. They're content. Real journalism requires training, ethics, editorial standards.

**Destroy it:** Two moves.

First: "You're right. This isn't replacing investigative journalism. This is replacing NOTHING. These communities have ZERO coverage. The alternative isn't The New York Times — it's a Facebook group post that disappears in 24 hours. We're strictly better than the status quo."

Second: Show a professional journalist's reaction. If a working journalist has tested the platform and given positive feedback, their endorsement answers this objection better than any argument. "Here's what a reporter at [publication] said after testing it."

---

## W4. "Contributors Won't Keep Contributing — It's a Novelty"

**The fear:** People try it once, then stop. The newspapers go dead.

**Destroy it:** Show the public records auto-generation pipeline. "Even if no human contributor ever touches the platform, the newspaper isn't empty. Council minutes, public notices, event calendars, and weather are generated automatically from public data. Human contributions ADD to an already-functioning publication."

Then show the behavioral precedent: "People already post local information for free — on Facebook groups, Nextdoor, Reddit, community WhatsApp. 2.3 billion Facebook posts per day, many about local events. The motivation exists. We channel it, we don't create it."

If you have tip-line data from PORT_: "We set up a tip line 20 hours ago. We're still receiving unsolicited tips. Nobody's paying these people. They want to share what they know."

---

## W5. "You Just Built a GPT Wrapper"

**The fear:** This is a thin shell around an API call. Anyone could replicate it in a weekend.

**Destroy it:** Show the full pipeline depth. Transcription → context assembly → article generation → multi-dimensional quality review → source verification → contributor feedback loop → revision cycle → publication → categorization → reader distribution → push notifications → analytics → community moderation.

"A wrapper makes one API call. We built a newsroom. The quality review alone runs 7 separate checks against the source material. The contributor feedback system creates a revision cycle that improves every article before publication. The reader site handles categorization, push notifications, and community discussion. The analytics layer tracks readership patterns across 100 municipalities."

Then: "If it's just a wrapper, explain the 100 live newspapers with real readers. Wrappers don't produce that."

---

## W6. "Legal Liability — Who Gets Sued When Something Goes Wrong?"

**The fear:** A defamatory article gets published. Who's responsible — the contributor, the platform, the AI?

**Destroy it:** "Same legal framework as every platform. Section 230 in the US, E-Commerce Directive in the EU. Contributors are responsible for their submissions. The platform provides tools — including AI review — but doesn't assume editorial control. This is the same model as YouTube, Medium, and every user-generated content platform."

Then add: "But we go FURTHER than those platforms. YouTube doesn't review your video before publishing. Facebook doesn't check your post for defamation. We do. Our AI review specifically flags potentially defamatory content, unverified accusations, and extreme factual claims. We're the only user-generated content platform that has a built-in editorial review layer."

---

## W7. "The Economics Don't Scale — API Costs Will Explode"

**The fear:** AI API costs are low now but grow linearly with usage. At 10,000 towns generating daily content, costs become prohibitive.

**Destroy it:** Show the math live. "This weekend: X articles, Y quality reviews, Z transcriptions. Total API cost: €12.40. That's €0.03 per article fully processed. At 10,000 towns generating 3 articles per day, that's €900/month. Running on Claude Haiku or a fine-tuned open model drops that to under €200/month."

Then: "AI costs drop ~40% per year. In 2024 this pipeline would have cost 5x what it costs today. In 2027 it will cost a fraction. We're building on a deflationary cost curve — the longer we run, the cheaper it gets."

Show the receipt. Real numbers kill hypothetical objections.

---

## W8. "Small Towns Can't Generate Meaningful Ad Revenue"

**The fear:** The revenue model is fantasy. A town of 2,000 people can't support a digital newspaper economically.

**Destroy it:** "A town of 2,000 doesn't need to support a newspaper. It needs to cover €5/year in hosting and AI costs. The local pizza shop paying €5/month for a featured listing covers that for the year. One advertiser. One town."

Then show the aggregation play: "But individual towns aren't the business model. 1,000 towns aggregated = regional ad inventory. 10,000 towns = national programmatic advertising. The business isn't one town making money. It's the network making money."

If a local business at PORT_ (catering, venue, any vendor) has expressed interest in advertising, mention it: "We already have an inquiry from a local business about advertising on the PORT_ newspaper. Day one."

## MATHEMATICALLY IMPOSSIBLE

These cannot happen in the time available. Period.

### I1. "100 Towns with Real Municipal Data"

The deployment is easy — wildcard subdomain, one template, dynamic routing. The DATA is the wall.

Finnish municipalities publish council minutes, but:
- Each municipality has its own website with its own structure
- Minutes are PDFs in different formats, different naming conventions
- Event calendars are in different CMS systems
- There's no centralized API for "all Finnish municipal decisions"

**The math:** Finding, downloading, and parsing public data for one municipality takes 15-30 minutes (find the website, locate the minutes section, download the right PDF, extract text). For 100 municipalities: **25-50 person-hours on data acquisition alone.** That's the entire team's weekend with zero time for anything else.

**What IS possible:** 10-20 towns with real municipal data. OR 100 towns with Wikipedia/general data (population, location, recent history) — weaker but technically "100 newspapers."

**The honest move:** Do 10-15 towns with REAL deep data (actual council minutes, actual local events). Show those. Say "we did 15 this weekend — the pipeline handles any municipality, we just need to point it at their public records." The 15 real ones are more convincing than 100 shallow ones.

### I2. "Covering Every Team at PORT_"

If there are 40 teams:
- Interview each team: 10-15 minutes per interview
- Total interview time: 7-10 hours of Samu's time
- Plus walking between teams, context switching, re-recording

That's Samu's ENTIRE Saturday doing nothing but interviews. He also needs to do the Mickos interview, judge interviews, tip-line management, voice from news desert, and his own reporting.

**What IS possible:** Cover 8-12 teams. Still impressive. "We covered a third of PORT_ in one day" is nearly as good as "every team."

### I3. "Meaningful Journalist Responses by Sunday Noon"

Email goes out Saturday morning. It's a weekend. Journalists check work email... Monday.

**Weekend cold email response rates: 2-5%.** Out of 200 emails, expect 4-10 responses by Sunday noon. Maybe fewer — journalists get a lot of email.

**What IS possible:** 4-10 responses is actually still powerful. "We emailed 200 journalists yesterday. 7 have already responded on a SATURDAY." The low absolute number is offset by the "on a Saturday" framing.

But "200 journalists responded" or "23 signed up on our waitlist" — mathematically very unlikely in 18 hours on a weekend.

### I4. "Media Coverage Published by Sunday"

News cycles on weekends are slow. Even if a journalist is interested:
- They need to verify the story
- They need editorial approval
- They need to write it
- Weekend editors are skeleton crews

A tweet or social media post? Possible. A published article on Yle or HS by Sunday? Essentially zero probability.

**What IS possible:** A journalist tweeting about it, or messaging "this is interesting, let's talk Monday." Frame it as "we have media interest" rather than "we have media coverage."

- Reaching an actual mayor (not an assistant, not voicemail): maybe 1 in 5
